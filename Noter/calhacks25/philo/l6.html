Miguel
Film: Rebel Without a Cause (1955)

Topic Focus

DeathLoveAdolescence

Miguel explores how teenagers cope with death in Rebel Without a Cause, examining the relationship between James Dean's character and the female lead as they navigate loss and mortality.

Philosophical Framework

Utilizing Heidegger's concept of "inauthentic" existence—the tendency to forget or deny one's mortality as a coping mechanism. The paper examines whether the characters engage authentically with death or suppress it.

💡 Professor's Guidance

Historical Context: Consider the significance of 1955 California culture. The film was made three years after the Korean War, during a period of affluence. The "rebel without a cause" reflects the inability of affluent youth to find meaning in a post-war society that no longer offered clear causes worth fighting for.

Technical Analysis Needed: Move beyond plot summary to analyze specific cinematographic techniques that convey the themes of death and love.

Areas for Development

  • Strengthen technical film analysis (cinematography, editing, mise-en-scène)
  • Explore the title's paradox: "Rebel Without a Cause" vs. the actual causes present
  • Identify substantial outside research sources
  • Connect to course readings on death and authenticity
Edward

DeathFaithMeaning

Edward analyzes the ideological contrast between the knight and his squire in their attitudes toward death and the search for meaning.

Philosophical Framework

The Knight: Upon realizing death is imminent, desperately searches for something to make his life meaningful—consistent with Heidegger's view that awareness of death can elevate one above day-to-day existence.

The Squire: Maintains a sarcastic, skeptical attitude toward death throughout—aligned with philosophers like Klages and Simmel who view death as simply the final stage of life's cycle, not something requiring special meaning.

Outside Research

David plans to outline Heidegger's concept of time and death, then respond critically to it, preferring Santayana's views on life and death.

Philosophical Inquiry

Central Questions

Religion & Authenticity: If God controls death, can one truly be "authentic" in Heidegger's sense? Does belief in divine control negate personal authenticity?

Suicide & Confrontation: Would suicide represent the ultimate confrontation with death, or does it contradict authentic existence?

📚 Professor's Guidance

Film Connection Essential: This must be a paper on philosophy in film, not purely philosophical theory. The Seventh Seal provides excellent material for exploring these questions through the knight's religious crisis and search for proof of God.

Myth Analysis: Consider two competing myths: (1) the religious myth that death is meaningful and leads to another world, and (2) the skeptical myth that only scientifically provable things merit belief. Both may be myths—poetic fictions that help us live.

Character Analysis

  • The Knight: Religious but craving proof of God's existence
  • The Squire: Scornful of religion after experiencing the horrors of the Crusades
  • Both perspectives are balanced against each other in the film
Catherine
Film: 8½ (1963) by Federico Fellini

Topic Focus

MythFantasyGender

Catherine identifies three main myths depicted through the protagonist's fantasies in Fellini's masterwork.

Three Mythic Structures

1. The Myth of the Ideal Woman

The virgin (his wife), the whore (his mistress), and the ideal woman (Claudia)—exploring the Madonna-whore complex and male fantasy projections.

2. The Myth of the Saraghina

Religious dimensions involving God, Satan, and the concept that "love is a sin"—examining Catholic guilt and sexuality.

3. The Harem Scene

The famous farmhouse sequence where all women dote on the protagonist. Interpretations include: (a) male fantasy of universal female devotion, or (b) Jungian anima—the women representing different aspects of his psyche.

💡 Professor's Interpretation

The Harem as Fantasy: This can be understood as a transcendent male daydream—a typical fantasy where all women love the protagonist despite his treatment of them. He possesses something valuable to them, and they dote on him as the only male in his harem.

Historical Context: This represents a male daydream from a time when women were more willing to tolerate such fantasies. Contemporary audiences may find it harder to accept, as women no longer need to "play that game."

Analytical Approach

Catherine is working with multiple scholarly sources, extracting relevant chapters from approximately five books on Fellini. The paper will explore how these myths relate to the protagonist's understanding and acceptance of love.

⚠️ Remember

Keep the dual focus on meaning and technique. How do Fellini's specific cinematic techniques convey these mythic structures? How is the meaning dependent upon the technical choices?

Kerry
Film: The Seventh Seal (1957)

Topic Focus

Character AnalysisAllegorySurvival

Kerry presents a sophisticated analysis of character development (or lack thereof) and the film's allegorical structure.

Central Thesis: Flat Characters

"There is no change at all in any of the characters. All the characters are extremely strong characters, and they're also flat throughout the whole entire movie."

Kerry argues that despite the knight's final meaningful action (saving the family), he remains static due to his lack of knowledge of God. This contrasts with traditional narrative expectations of character growth.

The Vision Paradox

Joff's Legitimized Visions

Early Vision: Joff (the actor/juggler) sees the Virgin Mary with Jesus—he has literally seen God.

Final Vision: At the moment when the knight commits his important action, Joff's visions are legitimized.

The Irony: If Joff had simply talked with the knight, the knight might have realized that God does make himself known at times. The answer was there all along.

Identification Through Ensemble

Since individual characters remain flat, Kerry suggests we identify through the entire cast of characters collectively, achieving growth through the ensemble rather than individual arcs.

The Chess Game Paradox

🎲 Structural Question

Kerry's Observation: "Death should be able to play a perfect game of chess. Chess doesn't really reflect life—you can't really win. Death always wins."

Professor's Response: What if we don't know that much about death? Perhaps death doesn't always win. The fiction allows for imagination beyond established truths. There's no "right way" in myth and fiction—you can imagine people defeating death.

Death's Cheating

Kerry notes that Death learns the knight's strategy by cheating (eavesdropping during the confession scene). The professor suggests this represents nature's "unscrupulousness"—nature has no concern for individual welfare, only for larger patterns of survival and continuance.

✓ Sophisticated Analysis

Kerry demonstrates advanced critical thinking by questioning narrative conventions and exploring the film's allegorical dimensions. The focus on form and structure shows strong analytical skills.

Felix
Films: Life is Beautiful (1997) and Amélie (2001)

Topic Focus

ImaginationMeaningEnhancement vs. Replacement

Felix explores how imagination adds or enhances meaning in life through two contrasting approaches.

Comparative Framework

Life is Beautiful: Imagination as Replacement

In Benigni's Holocaust drama, imagination almost replaces the meaning that would otherwise be present in life—particularly for the boy, but also for the adults. The father's storytelling gives meaning to their horrific circumstances, transforming the concentration camp into a "game."

Dual Function: The imaginative story protects the child while simultaneously giving the adults purpose and meaning in their own suffering.

Amélie: Imagination as Enhancement

Imagination doesn't replace ordinary life's meaning but enhances it. For the introverted Amélie, imagination helps her experience things she wouldn't otherwise experience, enriching rather than substituting for reality.

Technical Excellence

🎬 Cinematographic Analysis

Since Felix's first paper focused on sexuality in The Turn of the Screw, incorporating technical analysis in the second paper would demonstrate breadth.

Key Takeaways & Themes

  • Dual Dimensions: Every paper must address both technical (cinematographic) and meaningful (philosophical) dimensions of film.
  • Myth-Making in Cinema: Film functions as a myth-making art, creating poetic fictions that help us understand existence, death, love, and meaning.
  • Death as Central Theme: Multiple students engage with death—from Heidegger's authenticity to the allegorical representation in The Seventh Seal.
  • Character vs. Ensemble: Traditional character development may not apply in all films; sometimes meaning emerges through ensemble dynamics.
  • Imagination's Role: Imagination can either replace reality (as survival mechanism) or enhance it (as enrichment), both serving vital human needs.
  • Historical Context Matters: Understanding the cultural moment of a film's creation (post-war affluence, Cold War anxiety) illuminates its themes.
  • Research Depth Over Breadth: One or two well-analyzed sources are superior to superficial engagement with many texts.
  • The Seventh Seal's Richness: Bergman's masterwork offers inexhaustible material for exploring faith, doubt, death, art, and survival.

About The Seventh Seal

Film Details

Director: Ingmar Bergman

Year: 1957

Runtime: 96 minutes

Setting: Medieval Sweden during the Black Plague

Why This Film?

The Seventh Seal is considered by many to be one of the greatest films ever made, consistently appearing in top-10 lists for over fifty years. It exemplifies the course's central concerns: the intersection of philosophy and cinema, the nature of myth-making, and the technical means by which meaning is conveyed.

Central Themes

Philosophical Explorations

The Search for Meaning: The knight's quest for proof of God's existence represents humanity's need for transcendent meaning in the face of mortality.

Faith vs. Skepticism: The contrast between the religious knight and the cynical squire embodies competing worldviews about death, meaning, and the divine.

Art and Survival: The family of actors who survive suggests that art and creativity may offer a form of immortality that transcends physical death.

Death Personified: The allegorical representation of Death as a chess player creates a framework for exploring humanity's relationship with mortality.

🎓 Course Integration

This film connects to earlier course themes: the nature of myths (biblical myths in The Lady Eve, knowledge myths in The Turn of the Screw), the meaning of death (Heidegger's authenticity), and the relationship between technique and meaning that has been central throughout the term.
