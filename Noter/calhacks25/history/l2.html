The Question of Objectivity

A key discussion point emerged around whether quantitative data appears more "objective" because it's analytical and numerical, versus narrative approaches that seem more interpretive. However, as the discussion revealed, all historical methods involve interpretation and critical judgment.

"I think when you look at historical data presented in that fashion, it often comes across as being more objective because it's detached. It's something analytical. It's something quantitative."

üîç Critical Source Analysis

The Importance of Source Criticism

Ian's Observation on the Widows Study:

"What I thought was very noticeable was the way you turned a critical eye towards the data and conclusions you drew through that. It wasn't so much the fact that it was just using numbers and regressions, but that it was critical of how the data was being used."

Elements of Source Criticism

  • Origin and Purpose: Understanding why sources were created and for what audience
  • Biases and Limitations: Recognizing what might be missing or skewed in the record
  • Multiple Perspectives: Comparing different accounts of the same events
  • Methodological Transparency: Being explicit about how data was collected and analyzed

Case Study: The Coachman's Bare Rump

Jeffrey Ravel's piece demonstrates source criticism by examining multiple documents about the same incident - a police report and a case report three weeks later that tells a completely different story. This forces the historian to:

  • Compare conflicting accounts
  • Investigate why sources conflict
  • Consider the social position of those creating records
  • Reconstruct what "actually happened" while acknowledging uncertainty

The Challenge of Inadvertent Evidence

Both Ravel and the widows study demonstrate how historians often work with inadvertent evidence - sources created for purposes entirely different from the questions historians now ask of them.

Key Insight: The policeman investigating the incident at the Chevalier's house had no intention of speaking to issues of race, class, and social status in Ancien R√©gime France. Yet the historian can legitimately use this source to address exactly those questions - if done carefully and critically.

The Problem of Representation

Critical Question Raised:

If we have a large collection of preserved artworks showing "merry remarrying widows" cavorting with younger men, but quantitative evidence suggests widows were actually economically disadvantaged, which source tells us the "truth"?

The answer, as the discussion revealed, is that both sources tell us something true, but about different aspects of historical reality:

  • The images reveal cultural anxieties and social fears about female independence
  • The quantitative data reveals economic realities of widowhood
  • Together, they show the gap between representation and lived experience
Methodological Lesson: Visual images have such impact on us that we must be especially careful about their representativeness. What sticks with us may not be what was most common or most important in the past.

Meg Jacobs and Visual Propaganda

Miranda's Observation: "She had these pictures where it's like - I was sort of detached to how over the top propaganda was. And then you see these pictures, and they're just so comic nowadays. It really helped push the point of how strong these things were."

Jacobs' use of World War II propaganda posters demonstrates how visual evidence can make abstract historical arguments concrete and comprehensible, showing the intensity of government messaging about food policy and consumption.

üìä Quantitative vs. Cultural History: A False Binary?

Mutual Suspicions

Professor Ravel: "The cultural historians and the quantitative historians are quite mistrustful of each other. Quantitative historians assume anything that can't be quantified is problematic. Cultural historians suspect that numbers can be manipulated as easily as narratives."

The Power of Numbers

Miranda's Warning: "I think there's a real problem because people are like, well, the scientists said this, so it's true. If you put out a number, like 33% of people are this, people will almost automatically accept that."

The discussion highlighted how numerical data carries an aura of authority that can be misleading if not properly contextualized. Key concerns include:

Sample Size Issues

Reporting "63% of a sample" sounds authoritative - until you learn the sample size is nine people (as in Bynum's Holy Feast, Holy Fast).

Statistical Significance

Understanding whether observed patterns differ meaningfully from what we'd expect by chance.

Data Collection Bias

Being transparent about how sources were selected and what populations they represent.

Contextual Meaning

Numbers alone don't interpret themselves - they require historical context and judgment.

The Peer Review Process

Peter's Question: Are Historical Journals Refereed?

Yes - typically 1-2 referees for articles, 2-4 for books. The process is meant to be anonymous, though in small fields reviewers can often be identified. Referees check methodology, evaluate arguments, and assess contribution to the field.

The Revision Process: Authors typically receive critical feedback, think "what a complete idiot" the reviewer is, then gradually come to see merit in at least some criticisms. The process is iterative, with dialogue between authors, reviewers, and editors.

Beyond the Binary

The seminar ultimately suggested that the quantitative/cultural divide is less important than shared commitments to:

  • Source criticism - regardless of source type
  • Methodological transparency - explaining how conclusions were reached
  • Contextual understanding - situating evidence in its historical moment
  • Interpretive humility - acknowledging limitations and uncertainties

The MIT History Department, like most history departments, lacks comprehensive geographic and chronological coverage. The readings demonstrated:

Strong Coverage

American History: Colonial period through WWII and beyond, with multiple faculty covering different eras and approaches.

Spotty Coverage

Missing: Significant gaps in South American history, African history, Australian history, and various other regions and periods.

Specialization vs. Survey

The Curriculum Debate

Miranda's Concern: "I would really love to have a basic thing of English history, of Wales and Ireland and Scotland. A basic thing of one country's emergence. I feel like there's a step between the Plato to NATO classes and the very specific classes that's usually missing - a medium-sized version."

The tension between comprehensive coverage and specialized expertise reflects competing visions of historical education:

The "Plato to NATO" Problem: Comprehensive survey courses risk becoming superficial catalogs of dates and events, losing what makes history intellectually compelling. Yet without some breadth, students lack context for specialized studies.

Interdisciplinary Boundaries

History emerges as a uniquely porous discipline, collecting scholars at its margins who also belong elsewhere:

  • Economics and History - quantitative approaches to past economies
  • Political Science and History - institutional development and state formation
  • Literature and History - cultural texts and social meaning
  • Sociology and History - social structures and collective behavior
  • Biology and History - bioarchaeology, environmental history, disease
"History tends to be a discipline that picks up and also drops off lots of people at the margins. It's a discipline that tends to collect people who either belong here but also belong elsewhere, or belong elsewhere but can put a foot in here."

The Archive as Common Ground

Professor Ravel's Suggestion:

"One way we might talk about commonality is that for historians, the one necessary component of what we do is an archive. Each of us configures our archives in different ways. It isn't necessarily a brick and mortar building with yellowing papers that make you sneeze."

The concept of "the archive" extends beyond traditional document repositories to include:

  • Visual collections and propaganda materials
  • Skeletal remains and bioarchaeological evidence
  • Oral histories and recorded testimonies
  • Material culture and archaeological sites
  • Digital databases and quantitative records

‚è∞ The Relationship Between Past and Present

Why Do History?

Professor Ravel's Answer: "History is interesting and important because it offers a unique perspective on the present, on the moment that one lives in. The great merit of history is that contact with the past when you perceive both how similar it is - how ununique we are today - and yet at the same time when you perceive that people thought and did things in a very different way that we could never have conceived of."

This dual recognition - of continuity and difference - emerged as a central justification for historical study. History allows us to:

The Value of Historical Perspective

  • Recognize patterns that recur across time
  • Appreciate the contingency of present arrangements
  • Understand how current problems have deep roots
  • Imagine alternative possibilities by seeing past alternatives

John Dower and Contemporary Relevance

Nick's Report from Dower's Lecture: "He was talking about how intelligence got fouled up by our opinions of the Japanese - underestimated and misunderstood what we were going up against. And he connects that right to John Clark Connolly's race thing, saying the administration had this 1980s mindset that they were going up against nation states as opposed to terrorist groups."

Dower's work demonstrates how studying past failures of intelligence and understanding can illuminate present-day policy mistakes. The racism embedded in WWII-era thinking about Japan parallels contemporary misunderstandings of other cultures and conflicts.

The Bynum Problem

Caroline Walker Bynum's Holy Feast, Holy Fast attempts to connect medieval women's fasting practices to modern anorexia. The seminar found this connection more problematic than Dower's WWII-to-present links, raising questions about:

  • How much temporal distance is too much for direct comparison?
  • When do similarities become superficial rather than meaningful?
  • How do we account for radically different worldviews and belief systems?
The Challenge: Medieval women fasted for religious devotion within a comprehensive cosmological framework. Modern anorexics struggle with body image in a secular, medicalized context. Are these phenomena truly comparable, or does the comparison flatten important differences?

Between Anachronism and Antiquarianism

Professor Ravel's Proposed Framework: "I've toyed with writing an article called 'Between Anachronism and Antiquarianism.' The antiquarian cares not at all for the present and wants to lose themselves entirely in the past. The anachronist is incapable of appreciating past phenomena on their own merits. The good historian finds the balancing act between the two."

This framework suggests that historians must navigate between two dangers:

‚ö†Ô∏è Antiquarianism

Treating the past as completely separate, interesting only for its own sake, with no connection to present concerns or questions.

‚ö†Ô∏è Anachronism

Imposing present categories and concerns on the past, unable to see how people in other times thought differently.

The skilled historian maintains productive tension between these poles, using present concerns to generate questions while respecting past differences in answering them.

ü§î Is Pain Historically Constructed?

A Fundamental Question

The Professor's Challenge: "Is pain historically constructed or socially constructed? Is my experience of pain as somebody who lives in the 21st century the same or different than someone's experience of pain who lived in the 4th century?"

This question cuts to the heart of what historians can and cannot know about the past. It raises profound issues about:

  • Human universals - What aspects of experience remain constant across time?
  • Cultural mediation - How do beliefs and practices shape even physical sensations?
  • Historical empathy - Can we truly understand past experiences radically different from our own?
  • Biological constants - What role does unchanging human biology play in historical experience?

The Stakes of the Question

Why This Matters

How we answer this question shapes our entire approach to history. If we believe most human experience is biologically determined and constant, we can more easily make comparisons across vast time periods. If we believe experience is largely culturally constructed, we must work much harder to understand past differences.

The professor's colleague once said dismissively: "I suppose you're gonna try to tell me next that starvation is socially constructed." This sarcasm reveals the tension between:

Biological Realism

Some facts are just facts: death, starvation, pain. These biological realities transcend culture and provide stable ground for historical comparison.

Cultural Construction

Even seemingly biological experiences are mediated by culture: how pain is understood, expressed, endured, and given meaning varies dramatically across time and place.

Evidence from Bioarchaeology

The professor's reading of bioarchaeological literature suggests that pain probably is historically mediated. Evidence includes:

  • Skeletal evidence of people continuing to work with severe injuries
  • Medieval women enduring extreme gastrointestinal distress from prolonged fasting
  • Variations in pain tolerance and expression across cultures
  • Different cultural frameworks for understanding and responding to suffering
"If we think about the evidence recorded in bone structure - people cutting themselves up, flagellating themselves, starving themselves, having severe gastrointestinal pain - it's suggestive of modifications in the human experience, or at least modifications from one person to another."

Implications for Historical Method

This question has profound implications for how historians work:

If Experience is Largely Constant:

  • We can more confidently project our understanding backward
  • Comparisons across time periods are more straightforward
  • Present-day psychology and biology directly inform historical interpretation
  • The gap between past and present is narrower

If Experience is Culturally Mediated:

  • We must work harder to understand past differences
  • Comparisons require careful attention to context
  • Present categories may not apply to past experiences
  • The interpretive challenge is greater but potentially more revealing
The Unresolvable Tension: "This is a question we can't answer. We can argue about it. We can feel strongly and passionately about it. We can disagree completely, and both sides be completely convinced that they're right. But the truth of the matter is we can't settle it."

üéØ Key Takeaways

1. Methodological Pluralism

History encompasses diverse methodologies - quantitative, cultural, comparative, microhistorical - each with distinct strengths and limitations. No single approach has a monopoly on truth or objectivity.

2. Source Criticism is Universal

Whether working with numbers, images, texts, or material culture, historians must critically evaluate their sources: understanding their origins, recognizing biases, acknowledging limitations, and being transparent about methodology.

3. Visual Evidence Requires Special Care

Images have powerful emotional impact that can be both illuminating and misleading. Historians must question representativeness and avoid letting memorable images substitute for systematic analysis.

4. Numbers Need Context

Quantitative data carries an aura of authority but requires careful interpretation. Sample sizes, statistical significance, data collection methods, and historical context all matter enormously.

5. History as Porous Discipline

History's boundaries are unusually fluid, incorporating methods and practitioners from economics, political science, literature, sociology, biology, and other fields. This porousness is a strength, not a weakness.

6. The Archive Broadly Conceived

What unites historians may be less their subject matter or methodology than their shared commitment to working with "archives" - understood broadly as any systematic collection of evidence from the past.

7. Past and Present in Dialogue

History's value lies in creating dialogue between past and present, revealing both continuities and differences. The best history helps us understand ourselves while respecting past otherness.

8. Between Anachronism and Antiquarianism

Historians must navigate between imposing present categories on the past (anachronism) and treating the past as completely separate from present concerns (antiquarianism).

9. Temporal Distance Matters - But How?

Making connections between past and present may be easier with recent history, but distant history can reveal deeper structures and more radical alternatives. Both have value.

10. Fundamental Questions Remain Open

Core questions about historical experience - like whether pain is culturally constructed - cannot be definitively answered. Living with this uncertainty is part of the historian's craft.

Final Reflection: When historians from diverse specializations gather, they may not share subject matter, time period, or methodology. But they share a commitment to critical engagement with evidence, transparent argumentation, and the belief that understanding the past enriches our comprehension of the present - even when (perhaps especially when) the past proves strange and unfamiliar.

The Unanswered Question

"When disciplines are this fractured, how do they talk to each other?"

The seminar ended with this provocative question hanging in the air - a fitting conclusion that acknowledges both the challenges and the ongoing conversation that defines the historical profession.
