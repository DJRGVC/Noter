Lipids: A Brief Recap

Before diving into proteins, let's quickly review the key concepts from our previous lecture on lipids.

üîë Key Characteristics of Lipids
  • Rich in C-C and C-H bonds - predominantly hydrophobic
  • Hydrophobic (water-hating) = Lipophilic (fat-loving)
  • Amphipathic - containing both hydrophilic and hydrophobic components

Fatty Acid Terminology

Saturated Fatty Acids

Fatty acids with no carbon-carbon double bonds. All carbons are "saturated" with hydrogen atoms.

Unsaturated Fatty Acids

Fatty acids containing one or more double bonds. Double bonds create fixed geometry:

  • Trans - groups on opposite sides of the double bond (farther apart)
  • Cis - groups on same side of the double bond (closer together)

Phospholipid Membranes

Phospholipids are the fundamental building blocks of cellular membranes. These amphipathic molecules spontaneously assemble into bilayer structures that form the boundaries of cells.

üí° Semi-Permeable Membranes

Cell membranes are semi-permeable, meaning only certain molecules can pass through freely:

  • Can pass freely: O‚ÇÇ, H‚ÇÇO, small hydrophobic molecules
  • Need assistance: Charged molecules, large molecules, ions

Membrane Properties

‚ú® Self-Healing Property

Membranes are self-healing! When punctured (e.g., by microinjection), the non-covalent forces allow the membrane to reseal automatically once the needle is removed. This property is crucial for techniques like microinjection into cells.

Non-Covalent Forces at the Membrane Interface

  • Hydrogen bonding - between water and polar head groups
  • Electrostatic interactions - between charged head groups (positive and negative charges)
  • Hydrophobic interactions - among fatty acid tails in the membrane interior
ü§î Think About It: Why can't neurotransmitters like GABA (gamma-aminobutyric acid) cross membranes freely? Because they're charged! They require protein transporters to move across the lipid bilayer.

Amino Acids: The Building Blocks of Proteins

‚ö° Critical Fact

Proteins comprise 50% of all macromolecules in living organisms, making them one of the most important classes of biological molecules!

What Are Amino Acids?

Amino acids are called "amino acids" because they contain two key functional groups:

Amine Group (-NH‚ÇÇ)

Basic functional group

Carboxylic Acid (-COOH)

Acidic functional group

Central Carbon (Œ±-carbon)

Tetrahedral center

Side Chain (R group)

Variable component

Œ±-Amino Acids

The amino acids encoded in proteins are Œ±-amino acids, meaning the amine group is at the Œ±-position (directly adjacent) to the carboxyl group.

The 20 Encoded Amino Acids

There are 20 standard amino acids encoded by messenger RNA and incorporated into proteins during ribosomal biosynthesis.

üìã Important Note About Structures

You will always receive a handout with amino acid structures during exams. You do NOT need to memorize these structures! However, you SHOULD be able to identify their properties (hydrophobic, charged, polar, etc.) by looking at their chemical structures.

Classification by Side Chain Properties

1. Hydrophobic Amino Acids Non-polar

These amino acids have side chains rich in C-H bonds with minimal polar groups:

  • Glycine (Gly, G) - simplest amino acid, R = H
  • Alanine (Ala, A) - R = methyl group (CH‚ÇÉ)
  • Valine, Leucine, Isoleucine - branched aliphatic chains
  • Phenylalanine, Tryptophan - aromatic rings (still hydrophobic)
  • Methionine, Proline - special structural properties

2. Polar, Charged Amino Acids Charged

Positively Charged (Basic):

  • Lysine (Lys, K) - amine group in side chain (typically protonated)
  • Arginine (Arg, R) - guanidinium group (positively charged)
  • Histidine (His, H) - imidazole ring (can be charged)

Negatively Charged (Acidic):

  • Aspartate (Asp, D) - carboxylate group
  • Glutamate (Glu, E) - carboxylate group

3. Polar, Uncharged Amino Acids Polar

These contain polar groups capable of hydrogen bonding but are not charged:

  • Serine, Threonine - hydroxyl groups
  • Asparagine, Glutamine - amide groups
  • Tyrosine - phenolic hydroxyl (also aromatic)

Special Amino Acids

‚≠ê Noteworthy Outliers

Glycine (Gly, G): The simplest amino acid with no elaborate side chain (R = H). Its small size allows unique structural flexibility.

Proline (Pro, P): Unusual cyclic structure where the side chain connects back to the backbone nitrogen. This creates a rigid structure that's crucial for proteins like collagen.

Cysteine (Cys, C): Contains a thiol group (-SH) that can form disulfide bonds (S-S) with other cysteines. These disulfide bridges create covalent cross-links in folded proteins under oxidizing conditions.

One-Letter and Three-Letter Codes

Each amino acid has both a three-letter code (e.g., Ala) and a one-letter code (e.g., A). The one-letter codes are particularly useful for writing out sequences quickly.

üéÆ Fun Exercise: Try spelling your name using amino acid one-letter codes! Can you create a valid peptide sequence?

Peptide Bonds: Linking Amino Acids Together

Formation of Peptide Bonds

Amino acids are joined together through a condensation reaction (also called dehydration synthesis) that forms a peptide bond (also called an amide bond).

Peptide Bond Formation

The carboxyl group (-COOH) of one amino acid reacts with the amine group (-NH‚ÇÇ) of another, releasing a molecule of water (H‚ÇÇO) and forming a covalent C-N bond.

Key Properties of Peptide Bonds

üî¨ Critical Structural Features

1. Directionality: Peptides are ALWAYS written from N-terminus to C-terminus (left to right).

2. Restricted Rotation: The peptide bond has partial double-bond character, which restricts rotation around the C-N bond. This creates a planar, rigid structure.

3. Hydrogen Bonding Capacity:

  • C=O can act as a hydrogen bond acceptor
  • N-H can act as a hydrogen bond donor
‚ö†Ô∏è Important Convention

If you write a peptide sequence in the wrong direction, you're describing a completely different molecule! For example:

  • MIT (Met-Ile-Thr) ‚â† TIM (Thr-Ile-Met)

These are different peptides with different properties!

Backbone Structure

In a polypeptide chain, the repeating pattern of atoms forms the peptide backbone:

  • N - CŒ± - C - N - CŒ± - C - N - CŒ± - C...
  • Side chains (R groups) extend from the Œ±-carbons
  • Every third bond (the peptide bond) is rigid
  • Other bonds show free rotation
üí° Analogy: Think of a polypeptide as "spaghetti with little bits that haven't been cooked" - mostly flexible, but with rigid segments every few atoms!

Peptides vs. Proteins

Type Length Characteristics
Peptides 2-39 amino acids Shorter sequences, limited ordered structure
Small Proteins ~40-100 amino acids Can adopt stable folded structures
Proteins 100-2000+ amino acids Complex folded structures with specific functions

Hierarchical Organization of Protein Structure

Protein structure is organized in a hierarchical manner, with each level building upon the previous one. Understanding this hierarchy is crucial for understanding how proteins function.

1¬∞ Primary
2¬∞ Secondary
3¬∞ Tertiary
4¬∞ Quaternary
üéØ The Central Dogma of Protein Function

Primary Sequence ‚Üí Fold ‚Üí Function

  • The primary sequence encodes all information needed for folding
  • The three-dimensional fold determines the protein's function
  • It's the fold, not the sequence, that directly dictates function
‚ú® Amazing Fact

With just 20 amino acid building blocks, nature creates thousands of different protein structures with diverse functions: enzymes, structural proteins, transporters, receptors, antibodies, and more!

Primary Structure (1¬∞)

Primary Structure

The linear sequence of amino acids in a polypeptide chain, connected by covalent peptide bonds. This is the fundamental level of protein structure.

Key Characteristics

  • Defined by covalent bonds - peptide bonds link amino acids
  • Unique and specific - each protein has a defined sequence
  • Directional - always written N-terminus to C-terminus
  • Encodes folding information - contains all instructions for 3D structure
üß¨ Genetic Encoding

The primary sequence is determined by the genetic code in DNA, transcribed to mRNA, and translated by ribosomes. Each codon (3 nucleotides) specifies one amino acid in the sequence.

Heteropolymers

Proteins are heteropolymers - polymers made up of different monomer units (unlike homopolymers like starch, which are made of repeating identical units).

üîç Example: The sequence MATVLQR is completely different from RQLVTAM, even though they contain the same amino acids. Order matters!

Secondary Structure (2¬∞)

Secondary Structure

Regular, repeating structural patterns formed by hydrogen bonding between peptide backbone atoms (not involving side chains). The two major types are Œ±-helices and Œ≤-sheets.

‚ö° Key Point

Secondary structure is established exclusively by interactions between peptide bonds in the backbone (C=O and N-H groups), NOT by side chain interactions!

Œ±-Helix (Alpha Helix)

üåÄ Alpha Helix Characteristics
  • Right-handed spiral structure
  • Hydrogen bonds between C=O of residue n and N-H of residue n+4
  • 3.6 residues per turn
  • Side chains project outward from the helical axis
  • Cylindrical shape - compact and stable
  • First predicted by Linus Pauling through model building

The Œ±-helix is one of the most common secondary structure elements in proteins. All the hydrogen bonding potential of the backbone is satisfied within the helix structure.

Œ≤-Sheet (Beta Sheet)

üìÑ Beta Sheet Characteristics
  • Extended strands of polypeptide aligned side-by-side
  • Hydrogen bonds between adjacent strands
  • Pleated structure - side chains alternate above and below the sheet
  • Can be parallel or antiparallel:
    • Antiparallel: strands run in opposite directions (N‚ÜíC vs C‚ÜíN)
    • Parallel: strands run in the same direction (N‚ÜíC)
  • Strands can be far apart in primary sequence but close in 3D space

Œ≤-Turn (Beta Turn)

Short segments (typically 4 amino acids) that reverse the direction of the polypeptide chain. These are essential for connecting strands in Œ≤-sheets or helices, allowing the protein to fold back on itself.

Structural Motifs

Combinations of secondary structure elements create common motifs:

  • Helix-turn-helix - common in DNA-binding proteins
  • Œ≤-hairpin - two antiparallel strands connected by a turn
  • Greek key - complex arrangement of Œ≤-strands
  • Helix-loop-helix - found in many regulatory proteins
üé® Ribbon Diagrams

Protein structures are often shown as ribbon diagrams where:

  • Helices are shown as coiled ribbons or cylinders
  • Œ≤-strands are shown as arrows pointing C-terminus direction
  • Loops/turns are shown as thin lines or tubes

Tertiary Structure (3¬∞)

Tertiary Structure

The complete three-dimensional structure of a single polypeptide chain, including all secondary structure elements and the spatial arrangement of all atoms. Stabilized by interactions between amino acid side chains.

Forces Stabilizing Tertiary Structure

Unlike secondary structure (backbone only), tertiary structure involves all types of non-covalent interactions:

Hydrogen Bonds

Between polar side chains, side chains and backbone, or side chains and water

Electrostatic Interactions

Salt bridges between oppositely charged side chains (e.g., Lys+ and Asp-)

Hydrophobic Interactions

Clustering of nonpolar side chains in protein interior, away from water

Van der Waals Forces

Weak interactions between atoms in close proximity

Disulfide Bonds

Covalent S-S bonds between cysteine residues (special case)

üíß Hydrophobic Core

Most globular proteins have a hydrophobic core with hydrophilic residues on the surface. This arrangement:

  • Minimizes unfavorable water-hydrophobic interactions
  • Maximizes favorable water-hydrophilic interactions
  • Provides structural stability through hydrophobic collapse

Examples of Interactions

  • Side chain to side chain: Hydrogen bond between Ser-OH and Asp-COO‚Åª
  • Side chain to backbone: Hydrogen bond between Asn side chain and backbone C=O
  • Side chain to water: Hydrogen bonds between surface polar residues and solvent
  • Electrostatic: Salt bridge between Lys-NH‚ÇÉ‚Å∫ and Glu-COO‚Åª
  • Hydrophobic clustering: Leu, Val, Ile, Phe buried in protein core
üéØ Thermodynamic Stability

The native (folded) structure represents the thermodynamic minimum - the most stable state with maximum favorable interactions and minimum unfavorable interactions.

Common Tertiary Structures

  • All-Œ± proteins: Predominantly Œ±-helical (e.g., myoglobin, cyclin A)
  • All-Œ≤ proteins: Predominantly Œ≤-sheet (e.g., green fluorescent protein - GFP)
  • Œ±/Œ≤ proteins: Mixed secondary structures (e.g., triose phosphate isomerase - TIM barrel)
  • Œ±+Œ≤ proteins: Separate regions of Œ± and Œ≤ structure

Quaternary Structure (4¬∞)

Quaternary Structure

The arrangement of multiple polypeptide chains (subunits) into a larger functional complex. Each subunit has its own tertiary structure, and they associate through non-covalent interactions.

üìå Important Note

Not all proteins have quaternary structure! Many proteins function as single polypeptide chains. Quaternary structure only applies to multi-subunit proteins.

Types of Quaternary Structure

Homo-oligomers

Complexes made of identical subunits (e.g., four identical chains).

Hetero-oligomers

Complexes made of different subunits (e.g., two Œ± chains and two Œ≤ chains).

Hemoglobin: The Classic Example

üî¥ Hemoglobin Structure

Hemoglobin is the oxygen-transport protein in red blood cells with quaternary structure:

  • Composition: 2 Œ±-subunits + 2 Œ≤-subunits (hetero-tetramer)
  • Each subunit binds one heme group (contains iron)
  • Total capacity: 4 oxygen molecules per hemoglobin
  • Cooperative binding: Subunits communicate through conformational changes
‚ö†Ô∏è Clinical Significance

Single amino acid mutations in hemoglobin can cause serious diseases (e.g., sickle cell anemia). The quaternary structure is essential for proper oxygen transport - we'll explore this in detail in the next lecture!

Advantages of Quaternary Structure

  • Cooperative behavior: Subunits can communicate and regulate each other
  • Stability: Larger complexes can be more stable
  • Regulation: Binding sites at subunit interfaces provide regulatory opportunities
  • Efficiency: Multiple active sites in one complex
  • Error correction: Genetic economy - one gene can produce multiple functional units

Protein Folding: From Sequence to Structure

Protein Folding

The process by which a polypeptide chain spontaneously adopts its native three-dimensional structure. This is driven by the tendency to maximize thermodynamic stability.

The Folding Problem

One of the grand challenges in biochemistry is predicting the 3D structure of a protein from its amino acid sequence alone. This is difficult because:

  • Must consider all possible conformations (astronomical number)
  • Must calculate all non-covalent interactions simultaneously
  • Energy landscape is complex with many local minima
  • Computational cost increases dramatically with protein size
üßÆ Computational Protein Folding

Modern computational methods use molecular dynamics simulations to predict protein structures:

  • Ab initio methods: Predict structure from sequence alone
  • Success with small proteins: ~50-100 amino acids
  • Challenges with large proteins: Computational complexity increases exponentially
  • Recent advances: AI methods (e.g., AlphaFold) showing remarkable success

The Folding Pathway

Protein folding typically follows a pathway:

  1. Denatured state: Extended, unfolded polypeptide
  2. Collapse: Hydrophobic residues begin to cluster
  3. Molten globule: Compact but not fully organized
  4. Formation of secondary structure: Helices and sheets form
  5. Hydrophobic core formation: Critical stabilizing event
  6. Native state: Fully folded, functional protein
üé¨ GB1 Folding Simulation

The lecture included a computational simulation of GB1 protein folding. Key observations:

  • Starts as extended, denatured chain
  • Initial attempts show many "failed" conformations
  • Formation of hydrophobic cluster is a critical turning point
  • After hydrophobic core forms, structure rapidly converges to native state
  • Final structure has all secondary elements properly arranged

Factors Driving Folding

Hydrophobic Effect

Major driving force - burial of hydrophobic residues

Hydrogen Bonding

Stabilizes secondary and tertiary structure

Electrostatics

Salt bridges and charge-charge interactions

Entropy

Release of ordered water molecules from hydrophobic surfaces

üî¨ Experimental Note

Many small proteins can refold spontaneously after denaturation, proving that all information for folding is contained in the primary sequence (Anfinsen's principle).

Case Study: Collagen and Osteogenesis Imperfecta

üè• Clinical Relevance

This case study demonstrates how a single amino acid change can have devastating consequences for protein structure and human health.

Collagen: The Body's Structural Scaffold

ü¶¥ Collagen Facts
  • Most abundant protein in the human body
  • Provides mechanical support to tissues
  • Found in: bone, tendon, cartilage, skin, blood vessels
  • NOT an enzyme, transporter, or signaling molecule
  • Structural protein with specialized architecture

Collagen Structure

Collagen has a unique hierarchical structure:

  1. Primary structure: Rich in glycine (Gly), proline (Pro), and hydroxyproline
    • Repeating sequence: Gly-X-Y (where X and Y are often Pro)
    • Glycine at every third position is CRITICAL
  2. Secondary structure: Polyproline helix (NOT Œ±-helix)
    • Extended, left-handed helix
    • Proline's rigid structure enforces this conformation
  3. Tertiary structure: Three chains wind together
    • Forms a right-handed triple helix
    • Glycines face the interior (tight packing)
  4. Quaternary structure: Fibrils and fibers
    • Triple helices assemble into fibrils
    • Fibrils organize into fibers
    • Creates visible banding pattern in electron microscopy
üîç Why Glycine?

Glycine is the smallest amino acid (R = H). In the collagen triple helix, every third residue faces the interior where three chains come together. Only glycine is small enough to fit in this crowded space!

Osteogenesis Imperfecta (Brittle Bone Disease)

Osteogenesis Imperfecta

A group of genetic disorders (collagenopathies) caused by mutations in collagen genes, resulting in fragile bones that break easily.

The Molecular Defect

‚ö†Ô∏è Critical Mutation

Glycine ‚Üí Alanine substitution

Changing just ONE glycine to alanine (adding a single methyl group) in the collagen sequence causes:

  • Bulge in the triple helix - alanine's methyl group doesn't fit
  • Disrupted helix packing - three chains can't align properly
  • Propagation of defect - affects ALL fibrils containing that chain
  • Weakened structure - reduced mechanical stability

Clinical Consequences

  • Brittle bones that fracture with minimal trauma
  • Fractures in utero - bones break before birth
  • Irregular bone structure - visible on X-rays
  • Difficulty with birth - vaginal delivery may be impossible
  • Lifelong disability - bones break from normal activities
  • Variable severity - depends on which collagen gene and where mutation occurs
‚öñÔ∏è Social Impact

Children with osteogenesis imperfecta may have frequent fractures that can be mistaken for child abuse. Medical professionals must be aware of this condition to avoid false accusations against parents.

Lessons from Collagen

üéì Key Takeaways
  • Structure dictates function: Collagen's triple helix provides mechanical strength
  • Every amino acid matters: Single mutations can be catastrophic
  • Propagation of defects: One bad chain affects entire fibrils
  • Specialized sequences: Gly-X-Y repeat is essential for structure
  • Clinical relevance: Understanding structure helps explain disease
