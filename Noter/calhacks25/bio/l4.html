Protein Structure: A Brief Recap

The primary sequence of a polypeptide chain defines its folded structure. This folded structure is stabilized through various levels of organization:

1° Primary
→
2° Secondary
→
3° Tertiary
→
4° Quaternary
Secondary Structure

Non-covalent interactions between backbone atoms (α-helices, β-sheets)

Tertiary Structure

Interactions involving side chains with each other, the backbone, or water molecules

Quaternary Structure

Association of multiple polypeptide subunits into functional protein complexes

Quaternary Structure Examples

Proteins can form various multimeric structures:

  • Homodimers: Two identical subunits
  • Heterodimers: Two different subunits
  • Tetramers: Four subunits (e.g., hemoglobin: α₂β₂)
🔬 Hemoglobin Structure

Hemoglobin is a heterotetrameric protein consisting of two α-subunits and two β-subunits (α₂β₂). This quaternary structure is essential for its oxygen-carrying function.

Protein Denaturation

Proteins are held together by numerous weak forces. When subjected to certain treatments, these forces can be disrupted, causing the protein to denature (unfold).

Causes of Protein Denaturation

🔥 Heat

Breaks weak bonds, causing proteins to unfold and potentially aggregate irreversibly

Irreversible

⚗️ pH Changes

Alters charge states of amino acid side chains, disrupting electrostatic interactions

Potentially Reversible

🧪 Organic Solvents

Slip into hydrophobic cores and disrupt protein structure (>10% concentration)

Variable

💨 Shear Forces

Physical stress from narrow tubing or mechanical agitation

Physical
🍳 Real-World Example: Scrambled Eggs

When you scramble eggs, you're denaturing proteins through heat treatment. The unfolded proteins aggregate together, and this process is irreversible—you can't "unscramble" an egg!

Mechanism of pH-Induced Denaturation

pH changes affect protein stability by:

  • Protonating or deprotonating charged side chains
  • Disrupting salt bridges and electrostatic interactions
  • Converting attractive forces into repulsive ones
⚠️ Protein Misfolding Diseases

Several human diseases result from misfolded or aggregated proteins:

  • Alzheimer's disease: Amyloid-β aggregation
  • Mad Cow Disease: Prion protein misfolding
  • Parkinson's disease: α-synuclein aggregation

These are often neurological disorders caused by toxic protein aggregates.

Sickle Cell Anemia: A Molecular Disease

Sickle cell anemia is a classic example of an inborn error of metabolism or genetically linked disease. It demonstrates how a single nucleotide change can have profound consequences.

DNA mutation → mRNA change → Amino acid substitution → Protein dysfunction → Disease

🧬 One in 134 Million

The β-globin gene is located on chromosome 11, which contains approximately 134 million base pairs. A change in just one single base pair causes sickle cell anemia—a remarkable demonstration of the precision required for proper protein function.

Hemoglobin: Structure and Function

Red Blood Cells and Hemoglobin

Hemoglobin is the dominant protein in red blood cells (erythrocytes), which have unique characteristics:

  • No nucleus: Red blood cells eject their nucleus during differentiation
  • Terminally differentiated: Cannot divide or replicate
  • Lifespan: Approximately 100-120 days
  • Packed with hemoglobin: Maximizes oxygen-carrying capacity

Why Are Red Blood Cells Red?

The red color comes from the heme molecule, which contains iron (Fe²⁺). The heme group:

  • Binds to oxygen (O₂) in the lungs
  • Transports O₂ throughout the body
  • Exchanges O₂ for CO₂ in tissues
  • Returns CO₂ to the lungs for exhalation

Cooperative Oxygen Binding

Hemoglobin's tetrameric structure enables cooperative binding:

✨ Cooperativity Advantage

When one oxygen molecule binds to hemoglobin, it makes binding of subsequent oxygen molecules easier. This means:

  • All four binding sites fill within a narrow oxygen concentration range
  • Efficient oxygen loading in the lungs
  • Efficient oxygen release in tissues
  • Maximizes oxygen transport efficiency

Molecular Basis of Sickling

Normal vs. Sickle Hemoglobin

Normal Hemoglobin (HbA)

  • Tetrameric structure (α₂β₂)
  • Cooperative O₂ binding
  • Remains soluble in cells
  • Red blood cells maintain discoid shape
  • Smooth flow through capillaries

Sickle Hemoglobin (HbS)

  • Same tetrameric structure
  • Still binds O₂ cooperatively
  • Forms long fibrous aggregates
  • Cells become sickle-shaped
  • Blocks capillaries → pain

The Aggregation Mechanism

Hydrophobic Patch Formation

The valine substitution creates a "sticky patch" on the surface of HbS:

Protein-Protein Interaction PDB: 2HBS 
Sticky Patch on HbS tetramer #1:
    Val-6 (β-globin) - hydrophobic

Complementary Patch on HbS tetramer #2:
    Phe-85 (β-globin) - hydrophobic
    Leu-88 (β-globin) - hydrophobic

Result: Val-6 inserts into the Phe-85/Leu-88 pocket
         → Tetramers stick together
         → Long fibrous polymers form
         → Red blood cells deform
⚠️ Why Glutamate Doesn't Stick

In normal hemoglobin, glutamate at position 6 is negatively charged and hydrophilic. It would be energetically unfavorable to insert into the hydrophobic pocket formed by Phe-85 and Leu-88. The mutation to valine removes this electrostatic repulsion.

Alternative Mutations at Position 6

Consider what would happen with other amino acid substitutions:

Substitution Properties Predicted Effect
Glu → Asp Both negatively charged Normal No sickling
Glu → Lys Charged (opposite sign) Normal Repels hydrophobic patch
Glu → Ser Polar, small Mild Less hydrophobic interaction
Glu → Tyr Large, aromatic, somewhat hydrophobic Severe Likely to cause sickling

Evolutionary Advantage: The Malaria Connection

🦟 Heterozygote Advantage

Individuals who are heterozygous for the sickle cell trait (one normal gene, one mutant gene) have a significant advantage in malaria-endemic regions.

Genotypes and Phenotypes

Genotype Hemoglobin Malaria Resistance Disease Severity
HbA/HbA All normal ❌ Susceptible ✅ No sickle cell disease
HbA/HbS Mixed (50/50) ✅ Resistant ✅ Mild or no symptoms
HbS/HbS All sickle ✅ Resistant ❌ Severe disease

Clinical Evidence

Drug Trial Data (Plasmodium falciparum)

  • Normal individuals (HbA/HbA): 14 out of 15 infected (93%)
  • Sickle trait carriers (HbA/HbS): 1 out of 15 infected (7%)

Conclusion: Heterozygotes have ~13-fold reduced malaria infection rate!

Geographic Correlation

There is a striking overlap between:

  • Regions with high prevalence of sickle cell trait
  • Regions endemic for Plasmodium falciparum malaria
🌍 Evolutionary Selection

The sickle cell allele has been maintained in populations because the heterozygous advantage outweighs the disadvantage of homozygous disease. This is a classic example of balancing selection in human evolution.

Why Are Sickled Cells Resistant?

The malaria parasite (Plasmodium falciparum) has difficulty infecting sickled red blood cells because:

  • Altered cell shape makes invasion more difficult
  • Sickled cells are cleared more rapidly from circulation
  • Lower oxygen levels in sickled cells may inhibit parasite growth
  • Altered membrane properties affect parasite attachment

Introduction to Enzymes

Enzymes are the heavy lifters of the protein world—they catalyze virtually all reactions in metabolism and biosynthesis.

Enzyme

A protein-based biological catalyst that accelerates chemical reactions without being consumed in the process.

Enzyme Nomenclature

The "-ase" Suffix

Enzymes are typically named with the suffix -ase, often indicating their function:

  • Protease: Hydrolyzes proteins
  • Lipase: Hydrolyzes lipids
  • DNase: Hydrolyzes DNA
  • Ribonuclease: Hydrolyzes RNA
  • Oxidoreductase: Catalyzes oxidation-reduction reactions

Related Terms

Isozyme (Isoenzyme)

Different enzymes that catalyze the same reaction but are encoded by different genes

Different Genes

Allozyme

Variant forms of the same enzyme encoded by different alleles of the same gene

Same Gene, Different Allele

Why Do We Need Enzymes?

⏰ The Peptide Bond Problem

Consider the hydrolysis of a peptide bond:

  • Conditions: pH 7, 37°C, in water
  • Half-life without enzyme: ~600 years
  • With enzyme (protease): Milliseconds to seconds

Conclusion: Without enzymes, digesting a meal would take centuries!

Physiological reactions need to occur at:

  • Room temperature (~37°C)
  • Neutral pH (~7)
  • Aqueous environment

Many reactions are simply too slow under these conditions without catalysis.

General Enzyme Reaction Schemes

Enzyme Reaction Types General Schemes 
Single substrate → Single product:
    E + S ⇌ ES → EP ⇌ E + P

Two substrates → One product:
    E + S₁ + S₂ ⇌ ES₁S₂ → EP ⇌ E + P

One substrate → Two products:
    E + S ⇌ ES → EP₁P₂ ⇌ E + P₁ + P₂

Where: E = Enzyme, S = Substrate, P = Product

Thermodynamics and Kinetics

The Gibbs Free Energy Equation

ΔG = ΔH - TΔS

Where:

  • ΔG: Gibbs free energy change (kcal/mol or kJ/mol)
  • ΔH: Enthalpy change (heat content)
  • T: Temperature (Kelvin)
  • ΔS: Entropy change (disorder)
🤔 Why Focus on ΔG?

Enthalpy (ΔH) describes the total energy of all bonds in a molecule. However, in chemical transformations, we're not breaking down molecules to their constituent atoms—we're only changing specific bonds.

Gibbs free energy (ΔG) describes only the energy changes relevant to the reaction, making it the appropriate parameter for understanding biochemical transformations.

Two Key Questions

How Far Does the Reaction Go?

Determined by: ΔG (difference between substrate and product energy)

  • ΔG < 0: Reaction favors products
  • ΔG > 0: Reaction favors substrates
  • ΔG = 0: Equilibrium

How Fast Does the Reaction Go?

Determined by: Activation Energy (Ea)

  • High Ea: Slow reaction
  • Low Ea: Fast reaction
  • Enzymes lower Ea

Reaction Types

Exergonic Reaction

ΔG < 0: Products are at lower energy than substrates. Energy is released.

Example: Catabolic processes (breaking down molecules for energy)

Energy Released
Endergonic Reaction

ΔG > 0: Products are at higher energy than substrates. Energy is required.

Example: Anabolic processes (building molecules, storing energy)

Energy Required

Catabolic vs. Anabolic

  • Catabolic: Breaking down molecules → releases energy (exergonic)
  • Anabolic: Building molecules → requires energy (endergonic)

Memory tip: "Cata-" sounds like "catastrophe" (breaking down), "Ana-" sounds like "building up"

How Enzymes Catalyze Reactions

Fundamental Principles

✅ What Enzymes DO
  • Lower activation energy (Ea): Makes reactions faster
  • Remain unchanged: Can be used repeatedly (green chemistry!)
  • Increase reaction rate: By factors of 10⁶ to 10¹⁷
❌ What Enzymes DON'T DO
  • Change ΔG: Thermodynamics are unchanged
  • Shift equilibrium: Same equilibrium position, reached faster
  • Get consumed: Catalysts are recovered unchanged

Mechanisms of Catalysis

Enzymes use their three-dimensional structure to lower activation energy through several mechanisms:

1. Proximity and Orientation

Problem: Two substrates need to collide with correct orientation

Solution: Enzyme binds both substrates in adjacent binding sites with proper orientation

  • Increases effective concentration
  • Ensures proper alignment for reaction
  • Dramatically increases collision frequency

2. Strain and Distortion

Problem: Bonds are stable in their lowest energy conformation

Solution: Enzyme binding distorts substrate bonds

  • Twists bonds away from optimal geometry
  • Makes bonds more reactive
  • Example: Peptide bonds are most stable when planar—enzymes can twist them

3. Stabilization of Transition States

Problem: Charged intermediates are unstable

Solution: Enzyme active site stabilizes charged species

  • Provides complementary charges
  • Hydrogen bonding networks
  • Hydrophobic environment when needed

4. Acid-Base Catalysis

Amino acid side chains can donate or accept protons:

  • Acids: Asp, Glu (donate H⁺)
  • Bases: His, Lys, Arg (accept H⁺)
  • Facilitates bond breaking and formation

5. Covalent Catalysis

Enzyme forms temporary covalent bond with substrate:

  • Creates reactive intermediate
  • Example: Serine proteases form acyl-enzyme intermediate
  • Bond is later hydrolyzed to release product
🎯 The Active Site

The active site is a specialized region of the enzyme where:

  • Substrate binds (often with high specificity)
  • Catalytic residues are positioned
  • Microenvironment is optimized for reaction
  • Typically a small pocket or cleft in the protein

Enzyme Inhibition

Enzymes are common drug targets. By inhibiting specific enzymes, we can treat diseases without harsh conditions like heat or extreme pH.

Types of Enzyme Inhibitors

1. Competitive Reversible Inhibitors

Mechanism: Inhibitor binds to the active site, competing with substrate

  • Resembles substrate structure
  • Blocks substrate access
  • Can be overcome by increasing [substrate]
  • Reversible: Inhibitor can dissociate
Reversible Can be overcome
Competitive Inhibition Mechanism 
Normal reaction:
    E + S ⇌ ES → E + P

With competitive inhibitor (I):
    E + S ⇌ ES → E + P
    E + I ⇌ EI (inactive)

Add more substrate → shifts equilibrium toward ES

2. Irreversible Competitive Inhibitors

Mechanism: Inhibitor binds to active site and forms covalent bond

  • Permanently modifies enzyme
  • Cannot be displaced by substrate
  • Cannot be overcome by increasing [substrate]
  • Requires synthesis of new enzyme
Irreversible Cannot be overcome
Irreversible Inhibition Mechanism 
    E + I → E-I (covalently modified, inactive)

Adding more substrate does NOT help!
Only solution: synthesize new enzyme

3. Allosteric Inhibitors

Mechanism: Binds to site other than active site

  • "Allo-" means "other" or "different"
  • Causes conformational change
  • Reduces enzyme activity
  • Important for metabolic regulation
Non-competitive Regulatory

4. Allosteric Activators

Mechanism: Binds to allosteric site and increases activity

  • Induces favorable conformational change
  • Enhances substrate binding or catalysis
