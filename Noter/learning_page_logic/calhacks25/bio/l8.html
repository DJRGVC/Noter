DNA Replication: Bidirectional Process

Key Concept: Bidirectional Replication

DNA replication proceeds in both directions from each origin of replication, effectively doubling the speed of the replication process.

Circular vs. Linear DNA

üîµ Prokaryotic DNA

  • Structure: Circular
  • State: Usually supercoiled
  • Replication: Bidirectional from origin
  • Challenge: Collision point when replication completes

üü£ Eukaryotic DNA

  • Structure: Linear chromosomes
  • State: Wrapped around histones
  • Replication: Multiple origins
  • Challenge: Telomere end-replication problem

The Replication Fork

At each replication fork, several key processes occur simultaneously:

  • Helicase unwinds the double helix
  • Leading strand (pink) synthesizes continuously 5' ‚Üí 3'
  • Lagging strand synthesizes discontinuously in Okazaki fragments
  • Primase lays down RNA primers for lagging strand synthesis
‚ö†Ô∏è Critical Enzymes Required

After primer placement and DNA synthesis:

  1. Exonuclease removes RNA primers
  2. DNA Polymerase fills in the gaps
  3. DNA Ligase seals the nicks to create continuous strands

Replication Speed & Accuracy

Organism Type Speed (base pairs/second) Error Rate Reason for Difference
Bacteria ~1,000 bp/s Higher Short lifespan; rapid turnover acceptable
Eukaryotes 30-50 bp/s Lower Long-term genome integrity essential
üí° Why Speed Differences Matter

Bacterial Strategy: Bacteria divide quickly and have short lifespans. A few errors don't significantly impact the population, though they can lead to antibiotic resistance.

Eukaryotic Strategy: Eukaryotes must preserve genome integrity over long lifespans. Slower, more accurate replication is essential for maintaining cellular function across decades.

Basal Error Rate

Without any correction mechanisms, DNA polymerase has an inherent error rate:

Base Error Rate: 1 in 10¬≥ (1 in 1,000)

This means that for every 1,000 nucleotides incorporated, approximately 1 will be incorrect based purely on thermodynamic considerations.

Why errors occur: The energy difference between incorporating the correct vs. incorrect base is small enough that mistakes happen statistically.

Why this is problematic: With 3 billion base pairs in the human genome, this would result in 3 million errors per replication cycle‚Äîcompletely unacceptable!

Proofreading Mechanisms

3' ‚Üí 5' Exonuclease Activity

DNA polymerase possesses a remarkable built-in quality control mechanism: it can "look back" at the nucleotide it just added and verify its correctness.

How Proofreading Works

  1. Synthesis: DNA polymerase adds a nucleotide (e.g., attempting to add G opposite C)
  2. Verification: The enzyme checks the most recently added base
  3. Detection: If an incorrect base is detected (e.g., T instead of G)
  4. Excision: The 3' ‚Üí 5' exonuclease activity removes the incorrect nucleotide
  5. Retry: DNA polymerase attempts to add the correct base again
‚úÖ Improved Accuracy

Error rate after proofreading: 1 in 10‚Åµ (1 in 100,000)

This represents a 100-fold improvement in accuracy! The proofreading mechanism reduces errors from 1 in 1,000 to 1 in 100,000.

‚ö†Ô∏è Limitations of Proofreading
  • Can only check the most recent nucleotide added
  • Only functions during active DNA synthesis
  • Cannot fix errors discovered after polymerase has moved on
  • Cannot repair damage that occurs after replication

Bidirectional Enzyme Activity

DNA polymerase is remarkable in its dual functionality:

  • Forward reaction: 5' ‚Üí 3' polymerase activity (adds nucleotides)
  • Reverse reaction: 3' ‚Üí 5' exonuclease activity (removes nucleotides)

This bidirectional capability allows the enzyme to both build and edit DNA strands in real-time.

DNA Repair Systems: Guardians of the Genome

üèÜ Nobel Prize 2015

The mechanisms for DNA repair were significant enough to warrant a Nobel Prize in Chemistry in 2015, recognizing the researchers who deciphered these critical cellular processes.

Why DNA Repair is Essential

Our DNA faces constant threats from multiple sources:

  • ‚òÄÔ∏è Sunlight (UV radiation): Causes thymine dimers and other photoproducts
  • ‚ò¢Ô∏è Ionizing radiation: Breaks DNA strands and modifies bases
  • üß™ Reactive chemicals: Polyaromatic compounds, alkylating agents
  • üî• Dietary factors: Compounds from grilled/barbecued foods
  • üß¨ Spontaneous damage: Deamination, depurination
Target Error Rate: 1 in 10‚Åπ to 10¬π‚Å∞

Through the combined action of proofreading and repair mechanisms, cells achieve an astounding accuracy of approximately 1 error per billion nucleotides.

Base Excision Repair (BER)

üéØ For Single Base Damage

Used when only one base is damaged or incorrect, but the DNA backbone remains intact.

BER Process:

  1. Recognition: Damaged base is identified (e.g., cytosine ‚Üí uracil deamination)
  2. Base Removal: Glycosylase enzyme flips the damaged base out of the helix and cleaves the N-glycosidic bond
  3. Sugar-Phosphate Removal: AP endonuclease removes the remaining sugar-phosphate
  4. Gap Filling: DNA Polymerase inserts the correct nucleotide
  5. Sealing: DNA Ligase seals the final nick
Process Flow 
Normal DNA:    5'---A-C-G-T---3'
               3'---T-G-C-A---5'

Damaged:       5'---A-C-U-T---3'  (C ‚Üí U deamination)
               3'---T-G-C-A---5'

After BER:     5'---A-C-G-T---3'  (Restored)
               3'---T-G-C-A---5'

Nucleotide Excision Repair (NER)

‚ö†Ô∏è For Major DNA Distortions

Used when damage creates a significant distortion in the DNA helix structure, such as thymine dimers from UV exposure.

Thymine Dimers: A Common UV-Induced Lesion

When DNA is exposed to UV light, adjacent thymine bases can form covalent bonds with each other, creating a "dimer" that severely distorts the DNA structure. This cannot be fixed by simple base excision.

NER Process:

  1. Damage Recognition: Protein complexes identify helix distortion
  2. Dual Incision: Endonucleases cut the damaged strand on both sides of the lesion
  3. Excision: A segment of ~12-30 nucleotides is removed
  4. Synthesis: DNA Polymerase fills the gap using the complementary strand as template
  5. Ligation: DNA Ligase seals the remaining nick
üåô Xeroderma Pigmentosum (XP)

Genetic Disorder: Defective NER enzymes (inherited from both parents)

Consequences:

  • Extreme sensitivity to sunlight
  • Severe sunburns from minimal exposure
  • High risk of skin cancer
  • Eye damage from UV exposure
  • Neurological complications in severe cases

Management: Complete avoidance of sunlight; patients are sometimes called "Children of the Night" as they must reverse their schedules and remain covered head-to-toe when outdoors.

Key Repair Enzymes Summary

Enzyme Function Repair Type
Glycosylase Removes damaged bases BER
AP Endonuclease Removes sugar-phosphate backbone BER
Endonucleases Make dual incisions around damage NER
DNA Polymerase Fills gaps with correct nucleotides Both
DNA Ligase Seals final nicks Both

Telomerase & The End-Replication Problem

The Telomere Challenge

Linear chromosomes in eukaryotes face a unique problem: DNA polymerase cannot fully replicate the ends of chromosomes.

Why Chromosome Ends Are Problematic

Consider what happens at the end of a linear chromosome:

  • Leading strand: Synthesizes continuously to the very end (5' ‚Üí 3')
  • Lagging strand: Requires an RNA primer near the end
  • The problem: When the RNA primer is removed, there's no upstream DNA for polymerase to extend from
  • Result: A gap remains at the 5' end of the newly synthesized strand
‚ö†Ô∏è Consequence: Progressive Shortening

With each round of DNA replication, chromosomes would get slightly shorter. Over many cell divisions, this would eventually lead to loss of essential genetic information.

Nature's Solution: Telomeres

Telomeres are repetitive, non-coding DNA sequences at chromosome ends that act as protective "caps." They provide a buffer zone that can be shortened without losing important genes.

Telomerase: The Chromosome Extender

Telomerase Enzyme

A specialized reverse transcriptase that adds telomeric repeats to chromosome ends, counteracting the shortening problem.

Cell Type Differences:

üü¢ Stem Cells & Germ Cells

  • Telomerase: Active
  • Function: Maintain chromosome length
  • Reason: Must preserve genetic integrity across generations
  • Result: Unlimited replicative potential

üî¥ Somatic Cells

  • Telomerase: Inactive or low
  • Function: Telomeres shorten with each division
  • Reason: Limits replicative lifespan
  • Result: Cellular senescence (aging)
üèÜ Nobel Prize Recognition

The discovery of telomerase and its role in chromosome protection was awarded the Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine in 2009 to Elizabeth Blackburn, Carol Greider, and Jack Szostak.

Telomeres and Aging

The progressive shortening of telomeres in somatic cells is associated with:

  • Cellular aging: Cells reach the "Hayflick limit" and stop dividing
  • Tissue aging: Reduced regenerative capacity
  • Age-related diseases: Correlation with various pathologies
  • Organismal aging: Contribution to overall aging process
‚öñÔ∏è A Delicate Balance

Too much telomerase: Associated with cancer (unlimited cell division)

Too little telomerase: Premature aging and tissue dysfunction

Evolution has struck a balance that limits cancer risk while allowing sufficient cell divisions for a healthy lifespan.

Introduction to Transcription

What is Transcription?

Transcription is the process of copying genetic information from DNA into RNA. Like transcribing handwritten notes into typed text, the information content remains the same, but the format changes.

DNA ‚Üí RNA: Key Differences

Feature DNA RNA
Bases A, G, C, T A, G, C, U (Uracil replaces Thymine)
Sugar Deoxyribose (2'-H) Ribose (2'-OH)
Structure Double-stranded helix Single-stranded (can fold)
Stability Very stable (permanent record) Less stable (transient message)
Function Hereditary information storage Information transfer, catalysis
Location Nucleus (eukaryotes) Nucleus ‚Üí Cytoplasm
üí° Why the 2'-OH Matters

The presence of a hydroxyl group at the 2' position of ribose makes RNA:

  • More reactive: Can participate in catalysis (ribozymes)
  • Less stable: More susceptible to hydrolysis
  • More flexible: Adopts complex 3D structures
  • Transient: Perfect for temporary messages

Types of RNA

  • mRNA (messenger RNA): ~1-5% of total RNA; carries genetic information from DNA to ribosomes
  • rRNA (ribosomal RNA): ~80% of total RNA; structural and catalytic component of ribosomes
  • tRNA (transfer RNA): ~15% of total RNA; brings amino acids to ribosomes during translation
  • Other RNAs: Regulatory RNAs, small nuclear RNAs, etc.

In this lecture, we focus primarily on mRNA synthesis, with tRNA and rRNA to be covered in detail on Friday.

RNA Polymerase: A Simpler Machine

Good News: Transcription is Simpler!

Compared to DNA replication, transcription has several simplifications that make it more straightforward to understand.

Key Simplifications in Transcription

‚úÖ Major Advantages
  1. Selective copying: Only ~1.5% of the genome is transcribed (not the entire genome)
  2. Built-in helicase: RNA polymerase includes helicase activity
  3. No primer needed: Can initiate synthesis de novo
  4. Single strand: Only one DNA strand is transcribed (template strand)
  5. No Okazaki fragments: Continuous synthesis in one direction

RNA Polymerase vs. DNA Polymerase

DNA Polymerase

  • Requires separate helicase
  • Requires primer (RNA or DNA)
  • Uses dNTPs (dATP, dGTP, dCTP, dTTP)
  • Replicates entire genome
  • Creates leading and lagging strands
  • 3' ‚Üí 5' exonuclease (proofreading)

RNA Polymerase

  • Built-in helicase activity
  • No primer required
  • Uses NTPs (ATP, GTP, CTP, UTP)
  • Transcribes select genes only
  • Continuous synthesis (5' ‚Üí 3')
  • Lower fidelity (errors less critical)

RNA Polymerase Function

Location: Nucleus (in eukaryotes)

Direction: Synthesizes RNA 5' ‚Üí 3' (reads DNA template 3' ‚Üí 5')

Building blocks: Nucleotide triphosphates (NTPs)

üéØ Template vs. Coding Strand

Template strand (antisense): The DNA strand that RNA polymerase reads (3' ‚Üí 5')

Coding strand (sense): The DNA strand with the same sequence as the RNA (except T ‚Üí U)

The mRNA sequence is complementary to the template strand and matches the coding strand.

Natural Inhibitors: Œ±-Amanitin

‚ò†Ô∏è Deadly Mushroom Toxin

Source: Death cap (Amanita phalloides) and Destroying Angel mushrooms

Mechanism: Œ±-Amanitin is an allosteric inhibitor that locks RNA polymerase II in a closed, inactive state

Effect: Halts mRNA transcription, leading to cell death

Potency: Extremely toxic in minute quantities

Warning: Never consume wild mushrooms unless absolutely certain of identification!

Promoters & Gene Regulation

What is a Promoter?

A promoter is a DNA sequence located upstream (toward the 5' end) of a gene that serves as a binding site for transcription factors and RNA polymerase, initiating transcription.

The TATA Box

One of the most common and well-studied promoter elements in eukaryotes:

  • Sequence: TATAAA (or variations)
  • Location: Typically ~25-30 base pairs upstream of transcription start site
  • Function: Binding site for TATA-binding protein (TBP)
  • Role: Helps position RNA polymerase correctly
DNA Structure 
5'---[Upstream]---TATAAA---[Transcription Start Site]---[Gene]---3'
3'---[Upstream]---ATATTT---[Transcription Start Site]---[Gene]---5'
                    ‚Üë
                TATA Box

Transcription Initiation Complex

The process of assembling the transcription machinery:

  1. Recognition: Transcription factors recognize and bind to promoter sequences
  2. TBP Binding: TATA-binding protein binds to TATA box, bending the DNA
  3. Complex Assembly: Additional transcription factors bind sequentially
  4. RNA Pol Recruitment: RNA polymerase II is recruited to the complex
  5. Initiation: Transcription begins at the start site
üí° Why So Much Non-Coding DNA?

Much of the genome that doesn't code for proteins serves critical regulatory functions:

  • Promoters: Direct where transcription begins
  • Enhancers: Increase transcription rates (can be far from gene)
  • Silencers: Decrease transcription rates
  • Response elements: Allow genes to respond to signals

These regulatory regions act as "landing pads" and "control switches" for the transcription machinery.

üéØ Key Takeaways

1
DNA Replication is Bidirectional

Replication proceeds in both directions from each origin, doubling the speed. Bacteria replicate at ~1,000 bp/s while eukaryotes replicate at 30-50 bp/s for greater accuracy.

2
Multiple Error-Correction Mechanisms

DNA polymerase proofreading (3' ‚Üí 5' exonuclease) reduces errors from 1 in 10¬≥ to 1 in 10‚Åµ. DNA repair systems (BER and NER) further reduce errors to ~1 in 10‚Åπ.

3
Base Excision Repair (BER)

Fixes single damaged bases through glycosylase removal, followed by DNA polymerase and ligase action to restore the correct sequence.

4
Nucleotide Excision Repair (NER)

Removes larger segments (~12-30 nucleotides) when damage causes significant helix distortion, such as UV-induced thymine dimers. Defects cause Xeroderma Pigmentosum.

5
Telomerase Solves the End-Replication Problem

Active in stem and germ cells to maintain chromosome length. Inactive in somatic cells, leading to progressive telomere shortening associated with cellular aging.

6
Transcription is Simpler Than Replication

RNA polymerase includes built-in helicase, requires no primer, and only transcribes ~1.5% of the genome. It synthesizes RNA 5' ‚Üí 3' using NTPs (with uracil instead of thymine).

7
Promoters Direct Transcription

Promoter sequences like the TATA box serve as binding sites for transcription factors and RNA polymerase, determining where and when genes are transcribed.

8
RNA Structure and Stability

The 2'-OH group in ribose makes RNA less stable than DNA (perfect for transient messages) and allows it to adopt complex 3D structures essential for catalytic functions.
