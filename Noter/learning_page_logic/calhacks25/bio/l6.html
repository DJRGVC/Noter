Introduction to Nucleic Acids

🎯 Lecture Overview

This is the final biochemistry lecture before transitioning to molecular biology. We'll explore how nucleotides form nucleic acids, which are fundamental to information storage (DNA) and information transfer (RNA). This knowledge is essential for understanding the central dogma: DNA → RNA → Protein.

Nucleic acids represent the fourth major class of macromolecules we've studied, following proteins, lipids, and carbohydrates. What makes nucleic acids particularly fascinating is how their chemical structure directly enables their biological function—storing and transmitting genetic information.

Central Dogma of Molecular Biology

The flow of genetic information follows this pathway:

DNA → mRNA → Protein

This process involves multiple types of RNA: messenger RNA (mRNA), transfer RNA (tRNA), and ribosomal RNA (rRNA). All of these are polymers of nucleotides.

Understanding nucleic acid structure is critical because it reveals:

  • How genetic information is stored in a stable, readable format
  • How DNA can be replicated with high fidelity
  • How information is transferred from nucleus to cytoplasm
  • How regulatory processes control gene expression

What Are Nucleotides?

Nucleotide

A nucleotide is a molecular unit composed of three components: a five-carbon sugar (pentose), a phosphate group, and a nitrogenous base (nucleobase).

Nucleotides are the monomeric building blocks of nucleic acids. When we understand their structure, we can comprehend how they polymerize to form the macromolecules DNA and RNA.

🔬 Three Components of Nucleotides
  1. Carbohydrate: A five-carbon pentose sugar (ribose or deoxyribose)
  2. Phosphate: A phosphate group that forms phosphoester linkages
  3. Nucleobase: A nitrogenous base (purine or pyrimidine)

The Sugar Component

The carbohydrate component is a five-carbon pentose sugar. There are two types used in nucleic acids:

Ribose

RNA

Contains hydroxyl groups (-OH) on all carbons, including the 2' position. This makes RNA less stable and more reactive.

2'-Deoxyribose

DNA

Lacks the hydroxyl group at the 2' position (hence "deoxy"). This makes DNA more stable for long-term information storage.

Important Numbering System: The carbons in the sugar are numbered 1' through 5' (read as "one prime" through "five prime"). This prime notation differentiates sugar atoms from the atoms in the nucleobase, which use regular numbers (1, 2, 3, etc.).

The Phosphate Component

Phosphorus in nucleotides forms phosphoester bonds. In DNA and RNA polymers, phosphates occur as phosphodiesters, linking two sugar molecules together.

Phosphate Structure

Phosphorus forms 5 bonds total, with one oxygen typically carrying a negative charge. This negative charge makes DNA and RNA acidic molecules.

Important Nucleotides in Metabolism

Beyond their role in DNA and RNA, nucleotides serve critical functions in cellular metabolism:

⚡ Energy Currency Molecules
  • ATP (Adenosine Triphosphate): The primary energy currency in cells, containing three phosphate groups
  • GTP (Guanosine Triphosphate): Used in protein synthesis and signal transduction
  • Cyclic AMP (cAMP): A second messenger in cellular signaling pathways

Nucleobases: The Information Carriers

Nucleobases are the components that actually encode genetic information. There are two families of nucleobases, distinguished by their ring structure:

Purines

Two Rings

Adenine (A) and Guanine (G)

Larger molecules with a double-ring structure. Remember: small name, big structure!

Pyrimidines

One Ring

Cytosine (C), Thymine (T), and Uracil (U)

Smaller molecules with a single-ring structure. Remember: big name, small structure!

DNA vs RNA Bases

DNA contains: A T G C

RNA contains: A U G C

The key difference: Thymine (T) in DNA is replaced by Uracil (U) in RNA. Uracil is structurally similar to thymine but lacks a methyl group.

Hydrogen Bonding Patterns

The most important feature of nucleobases for understanding DNA structure is their hydrogen bonding capability. Each base has specific hydrogen bond donors (H attached to N or O) and acceptors (lone pairs on N or O).

🔗 Key Concept

You don't need to memorize the complete structures of all five bases, but you should understand where hydrogen bond donors and acceptors are located. This is crucial for understanding base pairing in the DNA double helix.

Nucleic Acid Polymers

Nucleotides join together through condensation reactions to form polynucleotide chains. This is the same type of reaction we saw in protein and carbohydrate synthesis.

Phosphodiester Backbone

The backbone of DNA and RNA consists of alternating sugar and phosphate groups, with bases extending from each sugar. The phosphodiester bonds connect the 5' carbon of one sugar to the 3' carbon of the next sugar.

Directionality: 5' to 3'

Critical Convention: DNA and RNA sequences are always written and read in the 5' → 3' direction. This is not arbitrary—DNA is also synthesized in this direction during replication!

For example, a sequence written as AGTC means:

  • 5' end starts with Adenine
  • Followed by Guanine, then Thymine, then Cytosine
  • 3' end terminates with Cytosine

Why "Nucleic Acid"?

📖 Historical Note

DNA was first isolated from white blood cell nuclei and was found to be highly acidic. The acidity comes from the phosphate groups in the backbone, which readily lose protons (H⁺) to become negatively charged (O⁻). Hence the name: nucleic acid = acidic material from the nucleus.

The Non-Covalent Structure of DNA

By the early 1900s, scientists understood the covalent structure of DNA—the sugar-phosphate backbone with attached bases. However, understanding how DNA stores and replicates information required discovering its three-dimensional, non-covalent structure.

Why Non-Covalent Structure Matters

The covalent structure alone doesn't explain:

  • How genetic information is encoded
  • How DNA can be replicated accurately
  • How the structure remains stable yet accessible

The answer lies in how two DNA strands interact through non-covalent forces.

Chargaff's Rules: The Critical Clue

In the early 1950s, biochemist Erwin Chargaff made a remarkable discovery that would prove essential to understanding DNA structure.

🔬 Chargaff's Discovery

After analyzing DNA from numerous organisms across all domains of life, Chargaff found that:

The ratio of purines to pyrimidines is always 1:1

More specifically:

  • The amount of Adenine (A) equals the amount of Thymine (T)
  • The amount of Guanine (G) equals the amount of Cytosine (C)

This finding was revolutionary because it suggested that purines and pyrimidines pair with each other in some systematic way. Even though different organisms had vastly different overall base compositions (some GC-rich, others AT-rich), this 1:1 ratio held constant.

Implication: DNA structure must involve pairing of a large base (purine) with a small base (pyrimidine), maintaining constant width throughout the molecule.

Discovering the Double Helix

Rosalind Franklin's X-ray Crystallography

The second critical piece of evidence came from Rosalind Franklin's groundbreaking work with X-ray diffraction.

📸 Photograph 51

Franklin developed techniques to create ordered DNA fibers that could be analyzed by X-ray crystallography. Her famous "Photograph 51" revealed:

  • DNA has a helical structure
  • The spacing between bases (3.4 Å)
  • The diameter of the helix (~20 Å)
  • The pitch of the helix (34 Å per complete turn)
⚠️ Historical Note

Rosalind Franklin tragically died of ovarian cancer at age 37, likely due to prolonged exposure to X-ray radiation during her research. Her contributions were essential to discovering DNA structure, though she did not receive the Nobel Prize awarded to Watson, Crick, and Wilkins in 1962 (Nobel Prizes are not awarded posthumously).

Watson and Crick's Model Building

Armed with Chargaff's rules and Franklin's diffraction data, James Watson and Francis Crick built physical models to deduce DNA's structure. They constructed large-scale models that allowed them to:

  • Test different base pairing arrangements
  • Verify that dimensions matched Franklin's measurements
  • Ensure chemical bonds were geometrically feasible
  • Explain how DNA could replicate
Competing Theory: Linus Pauling, fresh from his success with the α-helix structure, proposed a triple-stranded DNA model with bases on the outside. This was incorrect for multiple reasons, including massive electrostatic repulsion from closely packed phosphate groups. Pauling was distracted by his peace activism and nuclear disarmament work at the time.

Complementary Base Pairing

The key insight that explained Chargaff's data was complementary base pairing: specific purines pair with specific pyrimidines through hydrogen bonds.

A-T Base Pair

2 H-bonds

Adenine pairs with Thymine

Two hydrogen bonds form between complementary donor and acceptor groups on these bases.

G-C Base Pair

3 H-bonds

Guanine pairs with Cytosine

Three hydrogen bonds form, making G-C pairs more stable than A-T pairs.

Why This Pairing System Works
  • Constant width: Each pair consists of one purine (large) + one pyrimidine (small), maintaining uniform helix diameter
  • Complementarity: Each base has a unique partner, enabling accurate replication
  • Stability: Multiple hydrogen bonds hold strands together, yet allow separation when needed
  • Information storage: The sequence of one strand determines the sequence of the other

Antiparallel Orientation

The two strands of DNA run in opposite directions—they are antiparallel:

Antiparallel Strands

Strand 1: 5' → 3' direction

Strand 2: 3' → 5' direction (opposite)

This antiparallel arrangement is thermodynamically favored and creates the most stable hydrogen bonding geometry.

🔬 Experimental Evidence

Scientists have synthesized parallel DNA (both strands 5' → 3' in the same direction). Crystal structures show it's highly irregular and much less stable than antiparallel DNA. Nature chose antiparallel orientation because it's thermodynamically optimal.

Additional Stabilizing Forces

While hydrogen bonding between bases is crucial, it's not the only force stabilizing DNA:

🔗 Hydrophobic Interactions

The nucleobases are relatively hydrophobic, flat, aromatic structures. They stack on top of each other like steps on a spiral staircase, creating base stacking interactions. These hydrophobic forces contribute significantly to DNA stability.

Researchers have even created artificial DNA bases that lack hydrogen bonding capability but maintain the right size and hydrophobicity—these can still form stable DNA-like structures!

Major and Minor Grooves

The double helix creates two grooves of different widths:

  • Major groove: Wider groove where proteins can access base pair information
  • Minor groove: Narrower groove, also accessible to certain proteins

These grooves are important for protein-DNA interactions, particularly for transcription factors that regulate gene expression. We'll explore this further when discussing gene regulation.

Comparing DNA and RNA

While DNA and RNA are both nucleic acids, they have important structural and functional differences:

Feature DNA RNA
Sugar 2'-Deoxyribose (no OH at 2' position) Ribose (OH at 2' position)
Bases A, T, G, C A, U, G, C
Structure Double-stranded helix (primarily) Single-stranded with secondary structures
Stability Very stable, long-lasting Less stable, readily degraded
Function Long-term information storage Information transfer, catalysis, regulation
Location Nucleus, mitochondria Nucleus, cytoplasm, ribosomes
Types Genomic DNA, mitochondrial DNA mRNA, tRNA, rRNA, siRNA, miRNA, etc.

Why These Differences Matter

Functional Significance

DNA's stability: The absence of the 2'-OH group makes DNA much more resistant to hydrolysis. This is essential because DNA must maintain genetic information for the lifetime of a cell (or organism).

RNA's instability: The 2'-OH group makes RNA more reactive and easier to degrade. This is actually advantageous—RNA messages need to be temporary so gene expression can be regulated dynamically.

Structural diversity: RNA's single-stranded nature allows it to fold into complex three-dimensional structures, enabling catalytic activity (ribozymes) and regulatory functions beyond simple information transfer.

🧬 The RNA World

RNA's structural versatility has led to the "RNA World" hypothesis—the idea that early life may have relied primarily on RNA for both information storage and catalysis, before DNA and proteins evolved their specialized roles.

DNA Stability and Denaturation

Predicting DNA Stability

We can predict which DNA sequences will be most stable by counting hydrogen bonds:

🧮 Calculating Stability

Method:

  1. Count G-C base pairs (3 H-bonds each) = 3 × (number of G-C pairs)
  2. Count A-T base pairs (2 H-bonds each) = 2 × (number of A-T pairs)
  3. Add them together for total hydrogen bonds
  4. Longer sequences with more G-C content = more stable
Example: A sequence with 10 G-C pairs and 5 A-T pairs would have (10 × 3) + (5 × 2) = 40 hydrogen bonds. A sequence of the same length with 5 G-C pairs and 10 A-T pairs would have only (5 × 3) + (10 × 2) = 35 hydrogen bonds, making it less stable.

DNA Denaturation and Renaturation

One remarkable property of DNA distinguishes it from proteins:

Reversible Denaturation

When DNA is heated, the hydrogen bonds break and the two strands separate (denaturation). However, unlike proteins, when DNA is cooled slowly, the complementary strands find each other and re-form the double helix (renaturation or annealing).

Why this works: Base pairing is sequence-specific. Each strand serves as a template for the other, so they naturally reassociate correctly.

This property is exploited in many molecular biology techniques:

  • PCR (Polymerase Chain Reaction): Cycles of heating and cooling amplify DNA
  • Hybridization: DNA probes find complementary sequences
  • DNA sequencing: Controlled denaturation enables reading sequences

Drawing Complementary Strands

💡 Pro Tip

When asked to draw the complementary strand of a DNA sequence, it's easiest to:

  1. First sketch it "upside down" (antiparallel)
  2. Mark the 5' and 3' ends correctly
  3. Then fill in the complementary bases

This prevents confusion about directionality while determining base pairs.

DNA-Based Computing

Beyond its biological role, DNA's predictable base-pairing properties have inspired applications in nanotechnology and computing.

🖥️ Why DNA for Computing?
  • Nanoscale: DNA is a precisely structured material at the molecular level
  • Programmable: Base pairing rules allow predictable self-assembly
  • Parallel processing: Trillions of molecules can compute simultaneously
  • Information density: DNA can store enormous amounts of data in tiny volumes

DNA Origami

Scientists have developed techniques to fold DNA into predetermined shapes:

  • Design complementary sequences that will base pair in specific patterns
  • Create "staple strands" that hold longer DNA scaffolds in desired configurations
  • Build 2D and 3D nanostructures: smiley faces, boxes, tetrahedra, even tiny robots

DNA Nanostructures

By designing DNA sequences with specific complementarity patterns, researchers can create:

  • Cruciform structures (four-way junctions)
  • DNA tiles that self-assemble into larger arrays
  • Three-dimensional cages and containers
  • Molecular machines with moving parts

DNA Logic Gates

DNA can be programmed to perform logical operations:

Molecular Computing

DNA strands can be designed to implement Boolean logic:

  • AND gates: Output only when both inputs are present
  • OR gates: Output when either input is present
  • NOT gates: Output inverts the input

These can be combined to solve computational problems, with the answer "read out" as a specific DNA sequence that forms only when the logic conditions are met.

DNA Data Storage

DNA's information density makes it attractive for long-term data archiving:

  • One gram of DNA can theoretically store 215 petabytes of data
  • DNA is stable for thousands of years under proper conditions
  • Researchers have successfully encoded and retrieved books, images, and videos in DNA
The Future: As synthesis and sequencing costs decrease, DNA-based storage may become practical for archival data that needs to last centuries without requiring power or maintenance.

Key Takeaways

Essential Concepts to Remember

1. Nucleotide Structure

  • Three components: pentose sugar + phosphate + nucleobase
  • Ribose (RNA) vs. 2'-deoxyribose (DNA)
  • Prime numbering system (1' through 5') for sugar carbons
  • Phosphodiester bonds link nucleotides 5' to 3'

2. Nucleobases

  • Purines (2 rings): Adenine (A), Guanine (G)
  • Pyrimidines (1 ring): Cytosine (C), Thymine (T), Uracil (U)
  • DNA uses A, T, G, C; RNA uses A, U, G, C
  • Hydrogen bond donors and acceptors enable base pairing

3. DNA Double Helix

  • Two antiparallel strands (one 5'→3', other 3'→5')
  • Complementary base pairing: A-T (2 H-bonds), G-C (3 H-bonds)
  • Chargaff's rules: purine:pyrimidine ratio = 1:1
  • Stabilized by hydrogen bonds and base stacking (hydrophobic interactions)

4. DNA vs. RNA

  • DNA is stable (information storage); RNA is transient (information transfer)
  • DNA is double-stranded; RNA is typically single-stranded with secondary structure
  • RNA has diverse functions: mRNA, tRNA, rRNA, regulatory RNAs

5. Functional Implications

  • Complementarity enables accurate DNA replication
  • Sequence determines genetic information
  • DNA can denature and renature reversibly
  • G-C content affects stability (more G-C = more stable)
