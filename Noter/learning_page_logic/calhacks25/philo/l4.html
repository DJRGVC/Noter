Introduction & Literary Context

üéØ Central Question

How do we reconcile the accessible, popular medieval fantasy of Tolkien and Lewis with the disturbing, visceral medieval Christianity explored by Caroline Walker Bynum?

This lecture explores the fascinating intersection between medieval scholarship and 20th-century fantasy literature, focusing on how J.R.R. Tolkien and C.S. Lewis transformed their academic understanding of medieval culture into literary works that continue to resonate today.

üìñ The Narnia Series

Seven books exploring Christian themes through fantasy, with varying quality and accessibility for different age groups.

üó°Ô∏è The Lord of the Rings

Epic fantasy written as pseudo-history, complete with appendices and linguistic detail that blur fiction and chronicle.

üéì Academic Style

Both authors' literary styles closely mirror their scholarly work, creating dense, allusive narratives.

The discussion begins with an important observation: Lewis and Tolkien created a particular intellectual space between the World Wars that had enormous appeal to American audiences, despite being deeply rooted in British medieval scholarship.

Lewis's Medieval Model

In The Discarded Image, C.S. Lewis presents a comprehensive model of the medieval worldview characterized by three key features:

üìö Bookish

Medieval thinkers wanted to believe everything written by authorities, creating a vast web of textual knowledge.

üîÑ Systematic

They sought to reconcile all different sources into a coherent whole‚Äî"if there was an invention they would have appreciated, it was the card index."

üåç Eclectic

They drew from pagan, Christian, poetic, and historical sources without seeing contradiction.

On the imaginative and emotional level, it makes a great difference whether, with the medievals, we project upon the universe our strivings and desires or with the moderns, our police system and our traffic regulations.

‚Äî C.S. Lewis, The Discarded Image, p. 94

The Gravity Example

Lewis contrasts medieval and modern understandings through the example of a falling stone:

Medieval View:

If I'm a stone, I'm heavy, and I desperately want to be where I belong, which is on the earth. So if somebody drops me off a tower, I'm going to get as quickly as I possibly can back to earth.

Modern View:

The "gravity police" jump out and command the stone to fall according to mathematical laws‚Äîwe project our legal and regulatory systems onto nature.

‚ö†Ô∏è The Math Debate

Students debated whether mathematics represents absolute truth (capital-T Truth) or merely useful models and tools for describing observations. This philosophical question mirrors the medieval struggle to reconcile different authorities and systems of knowledge.

Christianity & The Medieval Model

Lewis makes a crucial and somewhat paradoxical claim: the medieval cosmology and religion were not easy bedfellows.

The cosmology appears to us in its firmly theistic basis and its ready welcome to the supernatural to be eminently religious. But it is not eminently Christian.

‚Äî C.S. Lewis, The Discarded Image, p. 18

This creates an important distinction:

üåå The Model

The cosmological model with its spheres, intelligences, and love-driven motion‚Äîused for poetry, allegory, and general cultural understanding.

‚úùÔ∏è True Christianity

Theology, spiritual advice, and homiletics‚Äîthe "real deal" that great spiritual writers focused on, often ignoring the model entirely.

üîó Connecting to Bynum

This distinction helps reconcile the comfortable, accessible medieval world of Tolkien's Middle-earth with the disturbing, visceral medieval Christianity explored by Caroline Walker Bynum. Middle-earth belongs to the model; Bynum's starving saints belong to eminently Christian spirituality.

The Silmarillion's Creation Myth

Tolkien's creation story exemplifies this medieval sensibility: God creates beings who sing the world into existence‚Äîan idea found in Boethius's Consolation of Philosophy, where celestial voices convey philosophy through song.

This explains Tolkien's fondness for lyric poetry throughout his work. The books don't literally sing, but they practically do, filled with poems that feel like they should have been heard before being read.

The Heroic Mode & Its Transformation

The lecture explores how World War I fundamentally challenged traditional notions of heroism, and how this crisis appears in both Tolkien's fiction and his scholarship.

üíÄ World War I's Impact

Unlike previous wars, WWI felt anti-heroic: machine guns replaced individual combat, soldiers gained inches rather than glory, and an entire generation was slaughtered without producing memorable heroes. As one student noted: "I can't name a single hero from World War I... except the Red Baron."

Classical Heroism: Beowulf's Death

The lecture provides an extended reading from Beowulf to illustrate the classical heroic mode:

Now is the time when I would have wanted to bestow this armor on my own son, had it been my fortune to have fathered an heir... For fifty years, I ruled this nation. No king of any neighboring clan would dare face me with troops... Go now quickly, dearest Wiglaf, under the gray stone where the dragon is laid out. I want to examine that ancient gold, gaze my fill on those garnered jewels. My going will be easier for having seen the treasure.

‚Äî Beowulf's dying speech

‚öîÔ∏è Heroic Death

The hero dies in battle, slaying a monster, with time for a final speech and the satisfaction of seeing his treasure.

üéñÔ∏è Glory & Praise

The warrior receives gifts, honor, and lasting fame for his deeds.

üëë Noble Lineage

Heroes are aristocrats, kings, men of distinction with family heritage.

But even Beowulf's ending contains anti-heroic elements: he dies childless, and a wailing woman laments that without him, all the old feuds will resurface. The Swedes and Danes will come. The people are doomed.

Lord of the Rings: The Anti-Hero

The lecture poses a crucial question: Who is the hero in The Lord of the Rings?

Student responses:

"Frodo." "Sam actually." "There's Aragorn‚Äîhe gets the girl and the kingdom."

The book actually presents two parallel narratives:

üëë The Heroic Story

Aragorn, Gimli, Legolas ‚Äî Traditional heroes engaging in battles, leading armies, achieving glory in the classical mode.

üåø The Anti-Heroic Story

Frodo and Sam ‚Äî Hobbits of no importance, trying to get rid of treasure rather than gain it, suffering without glory.

Frodo vs. Beowulf

Key Contrasts

  • Treasure: Beowulf gains it; Frodo destroys it
  • Status: Beowulf is a king; Frodo is a hobbit
  • Ending: Beowulf dies gazing at gold; Frodo must leave the Shire
  • Legacy: Beowulf leaves doom; Frodo leaves hope
  • Recognition: Beowulf receives praise; Frodo is the "unsung hero"

Frodo rescues the world for everyone else, but he couldn't rescue it for himself. Some wounds never heal. He saves the world, but he doesn't save what he really wanted to save.

‚Äî Student observation

The Christian Parallel

Multiple students noted the Christian themes in Frodo's story:

The Underdog Theme:

"It's the whole underdog thing. You have the person that no one else would choose taking on the task‚Äîlike Mary and Joseph. They get chosen even though they're at the bottom of the barrel. It's always the bottom of the barrel throughout Christianity."

This connects to Bynum's medieval women saints: they are God's ultimate agents precisely because they are low. The unexpected nature of their holiness is the point.

Tolkien's Choice

‚ú® The Significance of the Ending

Tolkien puts his cards on the table by how he ends the book. When you have two parallel stories and choose which gets the last word, you're making a powerful statement. The book doesn't end with Aragorn's coronation‚Äîit ends with Frodo leaving the Shire and Sam returning home to his family.

"Well, I'm back," Sam says‚Äîand this domestic, humble ending is what Tolkien chose to emphasize.

Beowulf & Anglo-Saxon Culture

The lecture emphasizes that understanding Beowulf is crucial to understanding both Tolkien's scholarship and his fiction.

The Poem's Structure

üè∞ Part 1: Denmark

Young Beowulf travels to help King Hrothgar, defeats Grendel and Grendel's mother, receives gifts and honor.

‚è∞ Time Jump

Fifty years pass. Beowulf becomes king of the Geats and rules peacefully.

üêâ Part 2: The Dragon

An old Beowulf must face a dragon awakened by a stolen cup. He dies in victory but leaves his people vulnerable.

The Peasant's Perspective

Student Question:

"Why is Beowulf so inspiring to a peasant if you're never going to be able to do it? I'm never going to be like Beowulf because of who I am."

Professor's Response:

"Maybe your expectation isn't that you're the one who's gonna do it. The story is appealing because somebody is gonna take care of you. If you're a peasant, you don't necessarily want to be Beowulf‚Äîit's not a good job description. Doom is certain."

The Anti-Heroic Elements

What came about brought to nothing the hopes of the one who had wrongly hidden riches under the rock face... It remains a mystery where his life will end when he may no longer dwell in the mead hall among his own... Yet Beowulf's gaze at the gold treasure when he first saw it had not been selfish.

‚Äî Beowulf, lines following the dragon fight

Even in this classical heroic text, there are anti-heroic notes: uncertainty about the afterlife, the doom awaiting the people, the mystery of death. The wailing woman's final lamentation emphasizes that without Beowulf, everyone will die.

World War Context & Historical Trauma

Both Tolkien and Lewis were World War I veterans, and this experience profoundly shaped their work.

üéñÔ∏è The War Generation

Their experiences in WWI made them less willing to live in the twentieth century. They were much more comfortable living in mental worlds they could control‚Äîthough notably, those worlds were still full of war and violence.

WWI Influences in Lord of the Rings

üíÄ The Dead Marshes

Bodies lying on their backs looking up‚Äîexactly how soldiers died in trenches.

‚öôÔ∏è Saruman's Industrialization

The "iron hand" of machinery versus traditional warfare‚Äîmirroring WWI's mechanized slaughter.

üè† The Unsung Hero

Frodo returns home to a Shire that doesn't understand or appreciate what he's done‚Äîlike WWI veterans.

Comparison with Braudel

The lecture draws parallels with historian Fernand Braudel, also shaped by war (WWII prisoner of war experience):

Both men saw "the horrors of a particular way of thinking about race." Braudel effaced race by emphasizing geography, climate, and material culture. Tolkien celebrated race through his different peoples (Elves, Men, Hobbits, Dwarves) but struggled with what that meant in the context of Nazi ideology.

The Germanic Ideal & Tolkien's Dilemma

This section addresses one of the most profound tensions in Tolkien's work: his love of Germanic culture in the age of Hitler.

I have spent most of my life since I was your age studying Germanic matters in the general sense that includes England and Scandinavia. There is a great deal more force and truth than ignorant people imagine in the Germanic ideal. I was much attracted by it as an undergraduate when Hitler was, I suppose, dabbling in paint and had not heard of it.

‚Äî Tolkien, letter to his son Michael, 1941

Tolkien's "Burning Private Grudge"

üî• Against Hitler

Tolkien wrote that he had "a burning private grudge" against "that ruddy little ignoramus, Adolf Hitler" for:

  • Ruining the noble northern spirit
  • Perverting Germanic ideals
  • Misapplying cultural heritage
  • Making forever accursed what Tolkien had loved and studied

Nowhere incidentally was it nobler than in England, nor more early sanctified and Christianized.

‚Äî Tolkien, 1941

The 1938 Letter to German Publishers

When German publishers asked if Tolkien was "Aryan" before publishing The Hobbit, his response was remarkable:

I am not aware that any of my ancestors spoke Hindustani, Persian, Gypsy, or any related dialects, and therefore cannot claim to be Aryan. However, if I am to understand that you are inquiring whether I am of Jewish origin, I can only reply that I regret that I appear to have no ancestors of that gifted people.

‚Äî Tolkien, 1938

The letter concluded with this devastating observation:

I have been accustomed to regard my German name with pride and continued to do so throughout the period of the late regrettable war. I cannot, however, forbear to comment that if impertinent and irrelevant inquiries of this sort are to become the rule in matters of literature, then the time is not far distant when a German name will no longer be a source of pride.

‚Äî Tolkien, 1938

üìÖ The Significance of 1938

This letter was written before the Sudetenland crisis, at a time when many "reasonable people" claimed they had no idea what was coming. Tolkien clearly understood what was happening in Germany and took a principled stand.

The Masculinist World

The lecture addresses criticism of Tolkien's male-dominated narratives:

The Feminist Critique:

Tolkien's world is utterly masculinist and violent. It's easy to write a feminist critique pointing out the lack of strong female characters.

Tolkien's Likely Response:

"You're right. It's a violent world. That is the world I know. This is the fallen world. It is violent. To the extent we can mitigate that violence‚Äîmaybe through female influence‚Äîthat's good. But read the sources. Read Beowulf. It'll tell you women aren't gonna solve this problem."

In Beowulf, the Danish queen is called a "peace weaver" multiple times, yet peace-weaving through marriage alliances consistently fails. The story of Ingeld shows this: married to his enemy's daughter to make peace, he ends up having to choose between killing his wife's father or his own brother.

üéØ Key Takeaways

Medieval Model vs. Christian Reality

  • Lewis argues the medieval cosmological model and true Christian theology were separate domains
  • The "comfortable" medieval world (spheres, humors, planetary influences) was the model
  • The "disturbing" medieval Christianity (Bynum's saints) was the real deal
  • Tolkien's Middle-earth belongs to the model; it's not "eminently Christian"

The Transformation of Heroism

  • Classical heroism (Beowulf): aristocratic warriors dying gloriously in battle, gaining treasure and fame
  • WWI destroyed this model: machine guns, trenches, mass slaughter without individual glory
  • Tolkien's response: the anti-hero (Frodo/Sam) who suffers without recognition, destroys rather than gains treasure
  • Christian parallel: the underdog chosen by God, the low made high

The Germanic Ideal Crisis

  • Tolkien devoted his life to studying Germanic culture and literature
  • Hitler's appropriation of Germanic ideals created a "burning private grudge"
  • Tolkien saw Hitler as ruining, perverting, and making "forever accursed" what he loved
  • His 1938 letter shows he understood Nazi ideology early and took a principled stand

Intellectual Context

  • Both Tolkien and Lewis were WWI veterans shaped by trauma
  • Both created mental worlds they could control, though still violent
  • Similar to Braudel: intellectuals responding to "a world gone mad" over race
  • Their fiction and scholarship are inseparable‚Äîboth are responses to historical crisis

Literary Significance

  • Tolkien wrote fiction as pseudo-history (appendices, languages, chronicles)
  • The line between fiction and history is deliberately blurred
  • His work has "enormous appeal" because it creates a believable alternate world
  • The ending matters: Tolkien chose the anti-heroic story (Frodo/Sam) over the heroic one (Aragorn)

üí≠ Final Reflection

These are "very intelligent men struggling with a world that is somewhat incomprehensible to them because it's gone mad in some very real sense." What they write‚Äîwhether fiction or history‚Äîmust somehow deal with living in a world gone mad, particularly over questions of race.

Tolkien was a mensch in the truest sense of that word. His intellectual and moral courage in 1938 demonstrates that it was possible to see what was happening and take a stand, even when many others claimed ignorance.
