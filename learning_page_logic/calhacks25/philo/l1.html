Philosophy in Film & Other Media

An Introduction to Film Aesthetics and the Philosophical Dimensions of Cinema

Course Introduction

Welcome to the first meeting of this course on Philosophy in Film and Other Media. This course explores the aesthetic dimensions of film and investigates how cinema can serve as a medium for philosophical inquiry. We'll examine how great filmmakers use their craft to explore profound questions about human existence, meaning, and value.

Course Approach

This course differs from traditional film theory courses. Rather than surveying the history of cinema or cataloging theoretical approaches, we'll engage deeply with specific films as works of philosophical art. Each film has been selected for its capacity to illuminate fundamental questions about human experience.

The structure of our meetings will typically involve preparation through readings, followed by film screenings and critical discussion. The goal is to develop your capacity to see films not merely as entertainment, but as sophisticated works of art that reward careful attention and philosophical reflection.

Twin Revolutionaries: Sturges & Welles

üé¨ Preston Sturges

Background: Originally a businessman and writer who revolutionized Hollywood by insisting on directing his own screenplays.

Innovation: Developed scripts through improvisation and dictation rather than traditional writing, creating a more organic, theatrical quality.

Specialty: Sophisticated screwball comedies that combined social satire with genuine warmth and humanity.

Legacy: Peaked quickly but influenced generations of filmmakers interested in combining intelligence with entertainment.

üé¨ Orson Welles

Background: Achieved worldwide fame at 23 with the "War of the Worlds" radio broadcast; arrived in Hollywood at 25 with unprecedented creative control.

Innovation: Produced, directed, wrote, and starred in Citizen Kane‚Äîa level of control unheard of for a first-time filmmaker.

Specialty: Dramatic films with innovative cinematography and narrative structure, often exploring themes of power and corruption.

Legacy: Created one of cinema's greatest masterpieces but struggled with Hollywood throughout his career.

Remarkable Parallels

Both filmmakers came from privileged backgrounds, received excellent European educations, had strong-minded mothers, and achieved their greatest work in 1941. Both were highly literate and brought intellectual sophistication to popular cinema. Both ultimately fell out of favor with Hollywood despite their genius. Their parallel careers illuminate the tension between artistic ambition and commercial demands in American filmmaking.

Learning to See: Film as Art

One of the primary goals of this course is to develop your capacity to see films as works of art. This requires training, just as appreciating music, painting, or literature requires cultivation. Film is a complex art form that combines visual composition, narrative structure, performance, sound, and editing into a unified aesthetic experience.

The Commentary Approach

Next week, we'll watch Marian Keane's commentary on The Lady Eve. This commentary is itself a work of scholarship that reveals dimensions of the film that might escape casual viewing. Keane brings a particular perspective‚Äîas a woman and as a student of philosopher Stanley Cavell‚Äîthat illuminates aspects of the film's treatment of gender, power, and romance.

However, I'll also be offering my own commentary on her commentary, pointing out where philosophical questions arise that deserve different or additional treatment. This layered approach‚Äîfilm, commentary, meta-commentary‚Äîmodels the kind of deep engagement with art that the course aims to develop.

Why This Method?

By experiencing the film first without guidance, then with expert commentary, then with philosophical reflection on that commentary, you'll develop a richer understanding of how meaning emerges in cinema. You'll learn not just what to think about The Lady Eve, but how to think about films in general.

Formalism vs. Realism: A Central Debate

Film theory has long been divided between two major approaches: formalism and realism. Understanding this debate is essential for appreciating how films create meaning.

Formalism
Emphasizes the technical and structural elements of filmmaking: cinematography, editing, composition, lighting. Formalists see film as an artificial construction that creates meaning through manipulation of its formal elements.
Realism
Emphasizes film's unique capacity to capture and reveal reality. Realists see film as an extension of photography's power to document the world, giving us access to reality in ways other arts cannot.
Hitchcock's "Pure Cinema"
The famous shower scene in Psycho exemplifies formalist principles: two minutes of screen time created from days of shooting, with nothing "really" happening‚Äîonly the artful assembly of shots creating psychological impact.
Bazin's "Total Cinema"
Andr√© Bazin argued that film represents humanity's age-old desire to capture reality completely. Film is the latest technology in a continuous effort to overcome the limitations of human perception.

Beyond the Binary

My approach in Reality Transformed (which you'll be reading) attempts to move beyond this either/or framework. Great films typically combine both formalist technique and realist capture of reality. The meaning emerges from the interaction between these dimensions‚Äîbetween the artificial construction and the reality it reveals.

Key Insight

Without technique, there is no meaning. Without meaning, technique is merely exercise. The two must work together. This dialectical relationship between form and content, between technique and meaning, is fundamental to understanding film as art.

Philosophy IN Film vs. Philosophy OF Film

It's crucial to understand the distinction between these two approaches, as it defines what makes this course unique.

Philosophy OF Film

This approach (which I taught last term) deals with abstract theoretical questions about the nature of film as a medium. What is the ontology of the photographic image? How does film create meaning? What distinguishes film from other arts? This is meta-level reflection on cinema itself.

Philosophy IN Film

This course takes a different approach. We're interested in how films themselves engage with philosophical questions‚Äînot questions about film, but questions about life, meaning, value, death, love, imagination, and human existence. We're treating films as works of philosophical art.

A Broader Conception of Philosophy

We're using "philosophical" in a broad sense‚Äînot the technical, specialized sense of academic philosophy departments, but the older, richer sense of fundamental questions about how to live and what matters. Great films explore these questions with a depth and sophistication that deserves to be called philosophical.

Why Films Can Be Philosophical

Until recently, many academics dismissed the idea that films could have genuine philosophical content. Film was seen as mere entertainment, not worthy of serious intellectual engagement. But this view fails to recognize several important facts:

  • Great filmmakers are often highly educated, literate individuals who think deeply about human existence
  • Film's combination of visual, narrative, and emotional elements gives it unique power to explore philosophical themes
  • Popular art forms can achieve genuine profundity‚Äîentertainment and depth are not mutually exclusive
  • Film can make philosophical ideas concrete and experiential in ways that abstract argument cannot

Film as Myth Making

The book I'm currently writing‚Äîwhich provides the intellectual framework for this course‚Äîis tentatively titled Film as Myth Making. This concept deserves careful explanation.

What Is Myth?

I use "myth" not in the colloquial sense of "falsehood," but in the richer sense of stories that express fundamental truths about human experience. The Bible, whether you believe it's divinely inspired or not, is great mythology‚Äîstories that have resonated across centuries because they touch something deep in human nature.

Myths vs. Scientific Theories

Myths differ from scientific theories in that they work through narrative and metaphor rather than abstract propositions. They're not concerned with literal truth but with existential truth‚Äîwith capturing and expressing what it feels like to be human, to love, to suffer, to hope, to die.

Classic Myths in Film

Throughout the semester, we'll examine how films engage with classic mythological themes:

Tristan and Isolde
The myth of forbidden love, explored through Jean Cocteau's adaptation. What happens when love conflicts with social duty? Can passion justify transgression?
Don Juan
The myth of the seducer, which has generated hundreds of versions since the 16th century. What does this figure reveal about desire, freedom, and mortality?
Pygmalion
The myth of transformation and creation, explored through Shaw's play and its film adaptations. What does it mean to remake another person? Who has that right?
Death and Life
Bergman's The Seventh Seal confronts mortality directly, asking what meaning life can have in the face of death. The most philosophical of all questions.

Teaching Philosophy: The Performing Artist

I want to be transparent about my approach to teaching, as it differs from what you might expect in a traditional course.

Born Teachers vs. Performing Artists

I distinguish between two types of educators. Born teachers have an intuitive ability to understand where students are developmentally and guide them through questioning and dialogue. They're focused on the student's growth and development.

Performing artists, by contrast, are focused on expressing their own understanding and vision. Like a painter approaching a canvas, they're not primarily thinking about their audience's needs‚Äîthey're working out their own ideas and making them available for others to engage with.

My Approach

I'm a performing artist, not a born teacher. What I offer is myself as an example‚Äîmy thinking, my struggles with these questions, my attempts to make sense of film and philosophy. Your task is to observe this performance critically, take what's useful, reject what isn't, and develop your own approach.

Why This Works at MIT

This approach is particularly effective with MIT students because you're exceptionally intelligent and capable of learning independently. You don't need someone to spoon-feed you information or guide you step-by-step. What you need is a sophisticated example to engage with‚Äîsomeone doing serious intellectual work that you can learn from, argue with, and ultimately surpass.

The goal is not to make you think like me, but to help you learn how to teach yourself. By watching someone engage seriously with difficult questions, you develop your own capacity for that kind of engagement.
