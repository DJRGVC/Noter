Introduction: Understanding the History Department

This lecture explores the fundamental question of what holds a history department together when its faculty members work across vastly different time periods, geographic regions, and methodological approaches. The discussion centers on how historians from various specializations approach their work and what common threads might unite them.

🎯 Central Question

How does a history department work when its members study everything from ancient cattle breeding to World War II propaganda, from French theater to Chinese frontiers, using methods ranging from quantitative analysis to cultural interpretation?

The diversity of these readings immediately raises questions about methodology, evidence, and what constitutes historical knowledge. Students noted the stark contrast between quantitative approaches (with regressions and statistical data) and cultural approaches (analyzing images, narratives, and social meanings).

Methodological Approaches in Historical Research

The Quantitative vs. Cultural Divide

Quantitative History

Uses statistical methods, demographic data, economic records, and mathematical analysis to identify patterns and test hypotheses. Often perceived as more "objective" because it deals with numbers and measurable phenomena.

Example: The widows study analyzing remarriage rates through probate inventories, using statistical significance tests to determine whether observed patterns exceed random chance.

Cultural History

Examines meanings, representations, symbols, and social practices. Analyzes texts, images, rituals, and cultural artifacts to understand how people made sense of their world.

Example: Dower's analysis of racial imagery in World War II propaganda, examining how visual representations shaped and reflected attitudes toward the Japanese.

Student Observation: "When you look at historical data presented in [quantitative] fashion, it often comes across as being more objective because it's something analytical. It's something quantitative."

⚠️ The Objectivity Question

Is quantitative history really more objective? Both quantitative and cultural historians must make interpretive choices: which sources to use, how to categorize data, what questions to ask, and how to present findings. Numbers can be manipulated as easily as narratives.

Critical Source Analysis

Being Critical About Sources

A key theme emerged around the importance of source criticism regardless of methodological approach. Historians must interrogate:

  • Origin: Who created this source and why?
  • Purpose: What was it originally intended to do?
  • Bias: What perspectives does it include or exclude?
  • Limitations: What can and cannot be learned from it?
  • Context: How does it fit into broader patterns?

The Widows Study: Source Criticism in Practice

The quantitative study was careful to note that its data came only from families where both parents had died, and only recorded second marriages if that spouse had also died. This selection bias could skew results, so the historian had to account for these limitations in the analysis.

The Coachman's Incident: Multiple Accounts

The historian didn't just rely on the initial police report. He found a second report three weeks later that told a completely different story, requiring careful comparison and analysis of why the accounts conflicted.

Ravel: "I think cultural historians suspect that numbers can be manipulated as easily as narratives and images in the service of biased history."

The Problem of Inadvertent Evidence

Many historical discoveries are accidental. Historians often find sources while looking for something else entirely. This raises important questions:

The police officer investigating the theatrical incident had no intention of speaking to issues of race, class, and social status in Ancien Régime France. Yet the historian uses his report for exactly that purpose. Is this legitimate?

The consensus: Yes, but with care. Sources weren't created for our purposes, but that doesn't invalidate their use. The key is understanding what the source can and cannot tell us, and being transparent about its limitations.

Visual Evidence and the Power of Images

Images as Historical Sources

📸 The Compelling Nature of Visual Evidence

Students noted that Dower's lecture was particularly powerful because of the graphics he used - propaganda images showing Hitler and Mussolini as tall, dignified figures while depicting Japanese as small, monkey-like caricatures. The visual evidence made the argument about racism immediately clear and visceral.

But this power creates problems:

  • Images are selective - we don't know if they're representative
  • Images are memorable - they stick with us more than text
  • Images are manipulative - they were often created to persuade
  • Images are culturally specific - we may misread their original meaning

The Merry Widow Problem

There exists a rich artistic and literary tradition of "merry widows" - wealthy women cavorting with younger men after inheriting their husband's fortune. These images are powerful and memorable. But quantitative evidence suggests most widows were actually economically disadvantaged. How do we reconcile these contradictory sources?

"The image, and our understanding of the image is something that speaks for itself... that has that potential to be so powerful that it speaks for itself. All I have to do is see that Hitler is tall and the monkey man is short, and there it is."

The Historian's Response

Rather than dismissing one type of evidence, the historian can ask: Why the discrepancy?

Perhaps society was afraid of wealthy, independent widows (even if rare) and ignored poor widows (even if common). The cultural sources reveal anxieties and fears, while the quantitative sources reveal material realities. Both are "true" in different ways.

The Authority and Danger of Quantitative Data

Why Numbers Seem Authoritative

Student Insight: "I think a lot of society is completely controlled by numbers... people are like, well, the scientists said this, so it's true... if you put out a number, like 33% of people are this, people will almost automatically accept that."

Numbers carry an aura of precision and objectivity. But this makes them potentially more dangerous than narrative sources because:

  • People are less likely to question statistical claims
  • Small sample sizes can be hidden (e.g., "63% of a sample size of nine")
  • Statistical significance can be misunderstood or misrepresented
  • The choice of what to measure shapes the conclusions

Statistical Literacy in Historical Analysis

The widows study addressed this by comparing observed patterns to what would be expected by chance. For example: "30% of pawns happened in wintertime" sounds significant, but winter comprises 25% of the year, so this is barely above random expectation.

⚠️ The Peer Review Process

Historical journals use peer review (typically 2-4 expert reviewers) to catch methodological problems, whether quantitative or qualitative. However, reviewers can disagree with each other, and the process is imperfect. The goal is not absolute truth but rigorous, well-supported arguments that advance scholarly discussion.

Narrative vs. Analysis: A False Dichotomy?

The Political Scientist's Critique

🔍 An Outside Perspective

A political science professor (Van Evera) reportedly claimed that historians are "not very useful for political scientists because they just do these specific events, and they're supposed to be individual and unique in history. Therefore, historians don't follow these big patterns."

This critique suggests historians are mere storytellers who don't engage in systematic analysis. But is this fair?

The MIT History Department's Response

Student Observation: "All the history classes I've had here do believe in making you think about, you know, if people act like this, why is it? And how does this affect later on?"

The MIT history faculty, according to students, emphasize analytical thinking over pure narrative. They ask:

  • What patterns can we identify?
  • What caused these events?
  • How do individual cases illuminate broader phenomena?
  • What can we learn that applies beyond this specific case?

Microhistory and the Particular

Even highly specific studies (like the coachman's incident) aim to illuminate broader questions about class, social structure, theater culture, and politics. The particular becomes a window into the general.

"Why do I care about this story? Not in a mean, snide way... but that's my reaction to the narrative."

Good history, regardless of method, must answer the "so what?" question. Why does this matter? What does it teach us?

The Past and the Present: Why Study History?

History as a Mirror

Ravel: "History is interesting and important... because it offers a unique perspective on the present, on the moment that one lives in. The great merit of history is that contact with the past when you perceive both how similar it is and how ununique and unoriginal we are today... and yet at the same time when you also perceive that people thought and did things in a very different way that we could have never conceived of in a million years."

This dual recognition - of similarity and difference - is what makes history valuable. We see ourselves reflected in the past, but also see alternatives to our present assumptions.

The Temporal Distance Problem

Does studying more recent history make connections to the present easier or more problematic?

Dower's World War II Analysis

Dower connects 1940s racism to 1980s intelligence failures and contemporary issues. This works because:

  • The same generation that grew up in the 1950s was making decisions in the 1980s
  • Racial attitudes persist across decades
  • The temporal gap is bridged by living memory

Bynum's Medieval Fasting Women

Bynum's connection between medieval religious fasting and modern anorexia is more controversial because:

  • Nearly a millennium separates the phenomena
  • The cultural contexts are radically different
  • No living memory connects them
  • The motivations appear fundamentally different (religious devotion vs. body image)
Yet some students found the connection compelling: "It's all about control, right? These women had no control of their lives. One thing they could control was their food. And with anorexics, it's the same thing."

Between Anachronism and Antiquarianism

✓ The Historian's Balancing Act

Antiquarian: Cares not at all for the present; wants to lose themselves entirely in the past

Anachronist: Incapable of appreciating past phenomena on their own merits; can only understand through the lens of the present

Good Historian: Finds the balance between the two, being "a little bit of the anachronist and a little bit of the antiquarian whenever necessary"

What Holds the History Discipline Together?

The Coverage Problem

The MIT history department has reasonable geographic coverage (American, European, Middle Eastern, East Asian history) and chronological range (ancient to contemporary). But it's missing significant areas (Africa, Australia, South America in depth).

Student Question: "I don't claim to be a full blown historian. Not yet at least. So there's a chance that I missed some grand chain that connect the works of some of the history faculty together. Because if there was one, I missed it."

Possible Unifying Factors

1. The Archive

Every historian works with an "archive" - though this might be:

  • Physical documents in a repository
  • Archaeological remains
  • Visual materials
  • Oral histories
  • Statistical databases

The common thread is working with primary sources from the past.

2. Critical Methodology

Regardless of whether they use numbers or narratives, all historians:

  • Question their sources
  • Acknowledge limitations
  • Build evidence-based arguments
  • Engage with other scholars
  • Revise interpretations based on new evidence

3. The Question-Driven Approach

Historians don't just collect facts; they ask questions:

  • Why did this happen?
  • How did people experience this?
  • What were the consequences?
  • How does this connect to broader patterns?
  • What does this tell us about human societies?

4. Interdisciplinarity

History is a "node" discipline that connects to many others:

  • Economics (economic history)
  • Political Science (political history)
  • Literature (cultural history)
  • Biology (bioarchaeology, environmental history)
  • Sociology (social history)

Like biology, history can claim relevance to everything because everything has a past.

The Institutional Reality

What actually holds a history department together may be more prosaic:

  • Credentialing: All have PhDs in history
  • Institutional needs: Coverage requirements for teaching
  • Shared training: Common graduate school experiences
  • Professional networks: Conferences, journals, scholarly communities
But there's also a deeper unity: the belief that understanding the past matters for understanding ourselves, our societies, and our possibilities for the future.

Key Takeaways

1. Methodological Pluralism: History encompasses both quantitative and qualitative approaches. Neither is inherently more "objective" - both require critical interpretation and careful source analysis.

2. Source Criticism is Universal: Whether working with numbers, images, texts, or artifacts, historians must interrogate their sources: Who created them? Why? What biases do they contain? What can and cannot be learned from them?

3. The Power and Danger of Evidence: Visual images are compelling but potentially misleading. Statistical data seems authoritative but can obscure small samples or questionable assumptions. Good historians are transparent about limitations.

4. History is Not Just Storytelling: While narrative is important, historians analyze patterns, test hypotheses, and make arguments. The goal is not just to describe what happened, but to explain why and what it means.

5. The Past Illuminates the Present: History matters because it helps us understand ourselves - both by showing us how similar we are to people in the past and how different. This dual recognition is history's unique contribution.

6. Disciplinary Identity is Complex: History departments are held together not by shared subject matter or method, but by a common commitment to understanding the past through critical engagement with primary sources and evidence-based argumentation.

7. The Balancing Act: Good historians avoid both antiquarianism (obsession with the past for its own sake) and anachronism (imposing present categories on the past). They maintain productive tension between past and present, particular and general, narrative and analysis.

8. Interdisciplinarity is Strength: History's connections to other disciplines (economics, political science, literature, biology) are not weaknesses but strengths. Everything has a past, making history relevant to every field of inquiry.
