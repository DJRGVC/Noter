Scale & Dimensions in Biology

⚠️ Important Unit: The Ångström

Chemists and biochemists frequently use the ångström (Å) as a unit of measurement. This can be confusing because:

  • 1 Å = 10-10 meters
  • 10 Å = 1 nanometer (nm)
  • Named after a Norwegian scientist
Unit Symbol Meters Common Usage
Picometer pm 10-12 Atomic radii, bond lengths
Ångström Å 10-10 Molecular dimensions
Nanometer nm 10-9 Proteins, DNA
Micrometer μm 10-6 Cells, organelles

Chemical Bonding in Biology

Understanding both covalent and non-covalent bonding is essential for biochemistry. Covalent bonds provide the structural framework of molecules, while non-covalent bonds give us dynamics—the ability to break and remake interactions readily.

Covalent Bonds

Strong & Stable

Form the backbone structure of biological molecules. These are the "permanent" connections.

~90-100 kcal/mol

Non-Covalent Bonds

Weak & Dynamic

Enable flexibility, folding, and molecular recognition. Can be broken and reformed easily.

~1-10 kcal/mol

Why Non-Covalent Bonds Matter

Non-covalent forces are essential for:

  • Forming the DNA double helix
  • Protein folding into functional 3D structures
  • Assembling lipid bilayers
  • Enzyme-substrate recognition

These weak forces can be broken relatively easily as long as you're ready to make another one in its place—this is the essence of biological dynamics!

Composition of Living Systems

💧 We Are Mostly Water

Living systems are approximately 75% water. This aqueous environment is critical—we survive in water, and all non-covalent forces operate in this context. The establishment of Earth's hydrosphere was essential for the evolution of life as we know it.

What's Left After Removing Water?

💡 Thought Experiment

If you placed a person in a giant desiccator and removed all the water, depending on their weight, about 40 pounds of material would remain.

Dry Mass Composition

Component Percentage Examples
Proteins ~50% Enzymes, structural proteins, antibodies
Carbohydrates ~25% Glucose, glycogen, cellulose
Nucleic Acids ~15% DNA, RNA
Lipids ~10% Membranes, hormones, energy storage
Small Molecules & Ions Small fraction Ca2+, Mg2+, Fe2+, metabolites
🔬 Why Start with Lipids?

Although lipids represent the smallest proportion of macromolecules, they are absolutely critical for life. The lipid bilayer membrane is as important as the genetic code—without cellular compartments, we couldn't concentrate reagents and perform biochemistry efficiently.

The Elements of Life

Good news for biology students: while chemists must worry about the entire periodic table, biological systems are built from a focused set of elements.

The Big Six Elements (98% of cellular mass)

  • Hydrogen (H) - Most abundant
  • Carbon (C) - Backbone of organic molecules
  • Nitrogen (N) - Amino acids, nucleic acids
  • Oxygen (O) - Water, functional groups
  • Phosphorus (P) - Energy storage, DNA backbone
  • Sulfur (S) - Protein structure, redox reactions

Additional Important Elements

Alkali & Alkaline Earth

Na⁺, K⁺, Mg²⁺, Ca²⁺

Critical for nerve signaling, muscle contraction, and enzyme function

Transition Metals

Fe, Zn, Cu, Mn

Essential for enzyme catalysis and electron transport

Trace Elements

I, Se, Mo, W

Required in tiny amounts for specific functions

🦋 Example: Iodine in Human Health

Where is iodine most prominent? The thyroid gland!

Iodine is a critical component of thyroid hormones (thyroxine) that regulate metabolism. We need trace amounts in our diet—too little causes problems, but too much is also harmful.

Covalent Bonding Basics

🔗 What Makes These Elements Special?

H, C, N, O, P, and S love to make covalent bonds. Unlike many metal ions that form ionic salts (like NaCl), these elements share electrons in stable covalent bonds—the foundation of all biological macromolecules.

Valence and Bonding Patterns

Element Neutral Bonds Lone Pairs Total Valence e⁻ Example
Carbon (C) 4 0 8 CH₄ (methane)
Nitrogen (N) 3 1 8 NH₃ (ammonia)
Oxygen (O) 2 2 8 H₂O (water)
Hydrogen (H) 1 0 2 H₂ (hydrogen gas)

🐰 Lone Pairs ("Bunny Ears")

Lone pairs of electrons are critical for biochemistry because they:

  • Serve as hydrogen bond acceptors
  • Can pick up protons (become positively charged)
  • Participate in nucleophilic reactions

Key insight: If you know where the lone pairs are, you know where hydrogen bonding can occur!

Charged States in Biology

Nitrogen at Physiological pH

At pH ~7-8, nitrogen's lone pair readily picks up a proton to become positively charged (e.g., NH₃⁺). This is the most common state for lysine side chains in proteins.

Oxygen Charge States

Oxygen can exist in three states:

  • Neutral: -OH (hydroxyl)
  • Positive: -OH₂⁺ (hydronium-like)
  • Negative: -O⁻ (alkoxide/carboxylate)

Special Cases: Phosphorus and Sulfur

Sulfur (S)

Good news: Sulfur copies oxygen!

Think of sulfur as oxygen's "older sibling"—the chemistry is very similar. You'll see -SH (thiol) groups behaving like -OH groups.

Phosphorus (P)

Different: Higher oxidation states

In biochemistry, phosphorus appears almost exclusively as phosphate (PO₄³⁻) with 5 bonds. Essential for ATP, DNA, and phosphoproteins!

Functional Groups in Biology

Functional groups are the "action sites" in biological molecules—places where chemistry happens. While large molecules may have many C-C and C-H bonds, functional groups are where the interesting biochemistry occurs.

📝 Notation: The "R" Group

Chemists use "R" to represent "anything else" in a molecule. For example, R-OH could be CH₃CH₂-OH (ethanol) or any other structure with a hydroxyl group.

Primary Functional Groups

Name Structure Charge State Importance
Hydroxyl R-OH Neutral Alcohols, sugars, serine
Carboxyl R-COOH Neutral (rare at pH 7) Amino acids, fatty acids
Carboxylate R-COO⁻ Negative (common!) Aspartate, glutamate
Amine R-NH₂ Neutral Amino acids
Ammonium R-NH₃⁺ Positive (common!) Lysine, arginine
Phosphate R-OPO₃²⁻ Negative ATP, DNA, phosphoproteins
Thiol/Sulfhydryl R-SH Neutral or R-S⁻ Cysteine, disulfide bonds

⚡ Electrostatic Interactions

When you know the charge states of functional groups, you can predict interactions:

Example: A positively charged ammonium group (R-NH₃⁺) can form an electrostatic interaction with a negatively charged carboxylate (R-COO⁻). This is called a salt bridge!

Composite Functional Groups

Some functional groups are formed by combining simpler groups through condensation reactions (releasing water):

Amide (Peptide Bond)

Formation: Carboxyl + Amine → Amide + H₂O

The bond that holds proteins together! So important it's called the "peptide bond" in proteins.

R-CO-NH-R'

Ester

Formation: Carboxyl + Hydroxyl → Ester + H₂O

Critical in lipids! Links fatty acids to glycerol in triglycerides and phospholipids.

R-CO-O-R'

Phosphate Ester

Formation: Phosphate + Alcohol → Phosphate Ester + H₂O

The backbone of DNA and RNA! Links nucleotides together.

R-O-PO₃²⁻-O-R'

⚠️ Special Note: Amide Nitrogen

Unlike regular amines, the nitrogen in an amide bond is not easily protonated. It tends to remain neutral because protonation disrupts the resonance stabilization of the amide bond. However, the N-H hydrogen can still participate in hydrogen bonding!

What You Need to Know

The information on functional groups presented here is all you need to know about organic covalent bonding for this course. There's a small amount of memorization required, but once you commit these structures to memory, you'll be in excellent shape for understanding biological molecules!

Non-Covalent Bonding

🌟 Why Non-Covalent Bonds Are Exciting

To me, non-covalent bonding is synonymous with dynamics—forces that can be readily broken and reassembled. This is the essence of how biological systems work!

Energy Comparison

Bond Type Energy (kcal/mol) Characteristics
Covalent Bonds 90-100 Strong, permanent, structural
Non-Covalent Bonds 1-10 Weak, dynamic, functional

The Power of Dynamics

Protein and nucleic acid structures are amazing because you can gradually break one non-covalent bond while making another. This allows:

  • Enzymes to change shape during catalysis
  • DNA to unwind and rewind
  • Proteins to fold and unfold
  • Molecular recognition without permanent attachment

All of this happens without the large energy barriers needed to break covalent bonds!

Types of Non-Covalent Interactions

1. Ionic Bonds (Electrostatic Interactions / Salt Bridges)

Energy: 2-10 kcal/mol (highly variable)

Description: Interactions between oppositely charged groups

Example: Protonated amine (NH₃⁺) ↔ Deprotonated carboxylate (COO⁻)

Why So Variable?

The strength of ionic interactions depends heavily on the environment:

  • In hydrophobic environments: Very strong (charges attract strongly)
  • In water: Weaker (each charge is solvated by water molecules)

When forming a salt bridge in water, both groups must give up their water solvation shells, which costs energy!

2. Hydrogen Bonds

Energy: 3-7 kcal/mol

Description: Sharing of a hydrogen between an electronegative donor and acceptor

🔍 Foolproof Method to Identify Hydrogen Bonds

  • Donors: H attached to O, N, or S (e.g., -OH, -NH, -SH)
  • Acceptors: Any atom with lone pairs (O, N with lone pairs)
  • NOT donors: C-H bonds (carbon holds on too tightly!)
💡 Recognizing Hydrogen Bonds

Hydrogen Bond Donors:

  • Hydroxyl groups: R-OH
  • Amine groups: R-NH₂
  • Amide groups: R-CO-NH-R'
  • Thiol groups: R-SH

Hydrogen Bond Acceptors:

  • Carbonyl oxygen: R-C=O (2 lone pairs)
  • Hydroxyl oxygen: R-O-H (2 lone pairs)
  • Amine nitrogen: R-NH₂ (1 lone pair)

Hydrogen Bonding in Proteins

Proteins are held together by amide (peptide) bonds:

-C(=O)-NH-

The C=O can be an acceptor, and the N-H can be a donor. Networks of these hydrogen bonds create protein secondary structures like α-helices and β-sheets!

3. Hydrophobic Interactions

Energy: Variable, often significant in aggregate

Description: Nonpolar molecules cluster together to minimize contact with water

🌊 The Oil and Vinegar Effect

Think of making salad dressing: when you shake oil and vinegar together, they quickly separate. The oil rises to the top because of hydrophobic interactions.

In proteins, hydrophobic amino acids (rich in C-H bonds) collapse away from water into the protein core. This drives protein folding!

Why Hydrophobic Interactions Are Special

Unlike hydrogen bonds and electrostatic interactions (which must compete with water solvation), hydrophobic interactions are favored in water. They don't have to "give up" water molecules—they're driven by the tendency of water to maximize its own hydrogen bonding network!

Result: Hydrophobic groups spontaneously cluster together, providing a major driving force for:

  • Protein folding
  • Membrane formation
  • Protein-protein interactions
4. Van der Waals Forces

Energy: 0.5-1 kcal/mol (very weak)

Description: Weak interactions between temporary dipoles in molecules

These are minor compared to the other three forces. Focus on electrostatic, hydrogen bonding, and hydrophobic interactions!

What You Need to Be Able to Do

  • Recognize and identify non-covalent interactions in complex molecular structures
  • Predict where hydrogen bonds can form based on donors and acceptors
  • Understand how charge states affect electrostatic interactions
  • Appreciate the role of hydrophobic interactions in aqueous environments
  • Explain why non-covalent bonds enable biological dynamics

Line-Angle Drawings

In biochemistry, we use simplified line-angle drawings to represent organic molecules. These are much easier to draw and read than full structural formulas.

Rules for Line-Angle Drawings

  • Each line represents a bond
  • Each vertex (corner or endpoint) represents a carbon atom
  • Hydrogens on carbon are implied (not shown)
  • Non-carbon atoms (O, N, S, P) are always shown
  • Hydrogens on heteroatoms (O, N, S) must be shown
  • Charges must be indicated
📚 Practice Makes Perfect

Line-angle drawings can seem confusing at first, but with practice, they become second nature. The rules are in your notes—spend some time practicing converting between full structures and line-angle drawings!
