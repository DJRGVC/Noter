Enzyme Energetics & Catalysis

Energy Diagrams and Activation Energy

Enzymes are biological catalysts that dramatically accelerate reaction rates by lowering the energy of activation (Ea). They achieve this by stabilizing transition states or high-energy intermediates.

Catalytic Power of Enzymes

Enzymes increase reaction rates by factors of:

10‚Å∂ to 10¬π‚Å∞ fold

This represents a million to ten billion-fold acceleration compared to uncatalyzed reactions!

Free Energy Changes (ŒîG)

Exergonic Reaction

A reaction with negative ŒîG where products are lower in energy than substrates. Energy is released, and the reaction is thermodynamically favorable.

Endergonic Reaction

A reaction with positive ŒîG where products are higher in energy than substrates. The reaction is thermodynamically unfavorable but can still proceed with catalysis.

Metabolic Classifications

Term Energy Change Process Type
Anabolic Endergonic (ŒîG > 0) Building/synthesis reactions
Catabolic Exergonic (ŒîG < 0) Breaking down/degradation reactions
Key Concept

Enzymes affect kinetics (reaction rate) by lowering activation energy, but they do NOT change the thermodynamics (equilibrium position) of a reaction.

Why Are Enzymes So Large?

Enzymes are typically much larger than their substrates‚Äîoften hundreds of times bigger. This raises an important question: why is all that extra protein structure necessary?

The Answer: Entire Structure Participates in Catalysis

While chemistry occurs at the active site, the entire enzyme structure collaborates to promote catalysis through:

  • Conformational dynamics and flexibility
  • Long-range electrostatic interactions
  • Substrate positioning and orientation
  • Product release mechanisms

Evidence for this comes from studying genetic mutations that affect enzyme function even when they occur far from the active site.

Case Study: Phenylketonuria (PKU)

Clinical Example

Phenylketonuria: A Genetic Enzyme Disorder

PKU is caused by mutations in phenylalanine hydroxylase, an enzyme that converts phenylalanine to tyrosine.

Phenylalanine ‚Üí Tyrosine
(via phenylalanine hydroxylase)

Why This Matters

  • Essential amino acid: Phenylalanine is necessary for protein synthesis
  • Toxic accumulation: Excess phenylalanine converts to toxic byproducts
  • Clinical consequences: Severe mental disorders and seizures if untreated

In phenylalanine hydroxylase (~4.9 nm diameter), disease-causing mutations occur throughout the protein structure‚Äî10-16 √Öngstr√∂ms away from the active site. This demonstrates that distant regions are critical for catalytic function.

Clinical Management

Newborn Screening & Dietary Management

PKU is one of several genetic disorders tested at birth. Early detection allows for:

  • Low-phenylalanine diet implementation
  • Prevention of toxic accumulation
  • Avoidance of artificial sweeteners containing phenylalanine

Aspartame (NutraSweet) is a dipeptide containing phenylalanine. Individuals with PKU must avoid diet drinks and products containing this sweetener, as they deliver high phenylalanine doses that cannot be metabolized properly.

The Equilibrium Problem

How do cells drive reactions forward when they're thermodynamically unfavorable (positive ŒîG)?

The Challenge

For an endergonic reaction:

Substrate ‚áå Product
(ŒîG > 0)

At equilibrium, there's mostly substrate and very little product. How do we get the product we need?

Solution: Reaction Coupling

Nature solves this by coupling reactions‚Äîlinking unfavorable reactions to favorable ones.

Mechanism

Sequential Pathway Coupling

A ‚Üí B ‚Üí C
(E1, ŒîG > 0)    (E2, ŒîG < 0)

How it works:

  1. Enzyme 1 (E1) catalyzes an unfavorable reaction: A ‚Üí B
  2. Enzyme 2 (E2) catalyzes a favorable reaction: B ‚Üí C
  3. As E2 consumes B to make C, E1 must produce more B
  4. This "pulls" the equilibrium forward through the pathway
Key Principle

By organizing reactions in sequential pathways and coupling unfavorable steps to highly favorable ones (often ATP-dependent), cells ensure metabolic flux in the desired direction.

Organization of Metabolic Pathways

Strategies for Pathway Organization

1. Co-localization

Enzymes in a pathway are physically grouped together:

  • Organellar localization: Confined to mitochondria, chloroplasts, etc.
  • Membrane association: Anchored to specific membrane systems
  • Protein complexes: Multiple enzymes in close proximity

2. Physical Association

Enzymes can be linked together:

  • Non-covalent interactions: Temporary enzyme-enzyme binding
  • Covalent linkage: Multi-domain enzymes with multiple catalytic activities on a single polypeptide chain

Advantages of Pathway Organization

  • Flux control: Ensures efficient forward progression
  • Toxic intermediate management: Prevents release of harmful intermediates
  • Regulatory efficiency: Coordinated control of multiple steps
  • Substrate channeling: Direct transfer between active sites
Example: Glycolysis

Aerobic glycolysis demonstrates reaction coupling perfectly. Early steps use ATP (unfavorable) to break down glucose. Later steps generate ATP through highly favorable reactions, resulting in net ATP production.

Feedback Regulation

Cells must regulate metabolic pathways to avoid overproduction of end products. The primary mechanism is negative feedback inhibition.

Regulatory Mechanism

Isoleucine Biosynthesis Pathway

Threonine ‚Üí [Intermediates] ‚Üí Isoleucine
                            ‚ä£ (feedback inhibition)

Mechanism:

  • When isoleucine accumulates, it binds to the first enzyme in the pathway
  • Acts as an allosteric inhibitor, reducing enzyme activity
  • Entire pathway slows down or stops
  • When isoleucine is depleted, it dissociates and the pathway reactivates
Notation Convention

In pathway diagrams, inhibition is shown as: ‚ä£ (perpendicular line to arrow)

This symbol indicates that the end product blocks the first enzyme's activity.

Why Inhibit the First Enzyme?

Stopping the first enzyme in a pathway is the most efficient regulatory strategy:

  • Prevents unnecessary consumption of starting materials
  • Conserves cellular energy (ATP)
  • Avoids buildup of pathway intermediates
  • Provides rapid on/off control

Requirement: For feedback inhibition to work effectively, pathway enzymes must be co-localized so that the end product can reach the first enzyme at sufficient concentrations.

Introduction to Carbohydrates

What Are Carbohydrates?

Carbohydrate

The term "carbohydrate" comes from early observations that glucose has the molecular formula C‚ÇÜH‚ÇÅ‚ÇÇO‚ÇÜ, which can be written as C‚ÇÜ(H‚ÇÇO)‚ÇÜ‚Äîliterally a "hydrate of carbon."

Note: Many carbohydrates don't follow this exact formula, but the name persists.

Biological Importance

Carbohydrates account for approximately 5% of cellular macromolecule mass and serve multiple critical functions:

  • Energy source: Primary fuel for cellular metabolism
  • Energy storage: Glycogen (animals), cellulose (plants)
  • Structural components: Cell walls, extracellular matrix
  • Cell signaling: Recognition and communication molecules
  • Nucleic acid components: Ribose and deoxyribose in RNA/DNA
Recognition Tip

How to identify carbohydrates in chemical structures:

  • Rich in C-OH bonds (hydroxyl groups)
  • Highly polar and water-soluble
  • Often contain carbonyl groups (C=O)
  • Names end in "-ose" (not "-ase" which is for enzymes!)

Naming Convention

Monosaccharides: Simple Sugars

Classification by Carbon Number

Trioses (3 carbons)

Example: Glyceraldehyde

  • Simplest carbohydrate
  • Contains aldehyde group and hydroxyl groups
  • Important intermediate in glycolysis

Pentoses (5 carbons)

Most important examples: Ribose and 2-Deoxyribose

  • Ribose: Component of RNA
  • 2-Deoxyribose: Component of DNA (missing one -OH group)
  • Form five-membered rings in nucleic acids
  • Essential for genetic information storage

Hexoses (6 carbons)

Most abundant and metabolically important carbohydrates

Key example: Glucose (C‚ÇÜH‚ÇÅ‚ÇÇO‚ÇÜ)

  • Primary energy source for cells
  • Building block of glycogen and cellulose
  • Exists in equilibrium between linear and cyclic forms

Structural Forms

Linear ‚áå Cyclic Equilibrium

Carbohydrates exist in equilibrium between open-chain and ring forms:

  • Pentoses: Form 5-membered rings (furanose)
  • Hexoses: Form 6-membered rings (pyranose)
  • In biological systems, the cyclic form predominates
  • Ring formation occurs through intramolecular reaction of carbonyl with hydroxyl

Glycosidic Bonds

Glycosidic Bond

The covalent bond that joins two sugar molecules together. Different linkage positions create different disaccharides with distinct properties.

Linkage Specificity

Same Monomers, Different Products

Two glucose molecules can be joined in different ways:

  • Œ±-1,4 linkage ‚Üí Maltose
    • Found in starch breakdown
    • Easily digested by humans
  • Œ≤-1,4 linkage ‚Üí Cellobiose (in cellulose)
    • Found in plant cell walls
    • NOT digestible by humans
  • Œ≤-1,4 linkage (Gal-Glc) ‚Üí Lactose
    • Milk sugar
    • Requires lactase enzyme to digest
Lactose Intolerance

Many adults lose the ability to produce lactase, the enzyme that breaks the glycosidic bond in lactose. This results in:

  • Inability to digest dairy products
  • Gastrointestinal symptoms
  • Can be managed with lactase supplements or lactose-free products

  • Structure: Highly branched polymer of glucose
  • Linkages: Œ±-1,4 (linear) and Œ±-1,6 (branch points)
  • Location: Primarily liver and muscle tissue
  • Function: Rapid glucose mobilization during energy demand

  • Linkages: Œ≤-1,4 glycosidic bonds
  • Location: Plant cell walls

Why Can't We Digest Cellulose?

Humans lack the enzyme cellulase needed to break Œ≤-1,4 glycosidic bonds. We can only break Œ±-linkages (as in glycogen and starch). This is why:

  • We can digest starch (Œ±-linkages) but not cellulose (Œ≤-linkages)
  • Cellulose passes through as dietary fiber
  • Herbivores rely on gut bacteria that produce cellulase

Glycans in Cell Biology

Glycan

A general term for any polymer of sugars (also called polysaccharide). Glycans serve diverse functions beyond energy storage.

Locations and Functions of Glycans

  • Extracellular Matrix: Meshwork of carbohydrate polymers surrounding cells
    • Provides structural support
    • Traps signaling molecules
    • Mediates cell-cell interactions
  • Protein Modifications: Sugars attached to proteins (glycoproteins)
    • Nuclear import/export signals
    • Protein stability and folding
    • Cell surface recognition
  • Lipid Modifications: Sugars attached to membrane lipids (glycolipids)
    • Cell surface markers
    • Cell-cell communication
    • Immune recognition

Blood Groups: Carbohydrates in Medicine

One of the most clinically important examples of carbohydrate diversity is the ABO blood group system.

The ABO Blood Group System

Blood Groups Are Defined by Surface Sugars

The four major blood groups (A, B, AB, O) differ only in the carbohydrates displayed on red blood cell surfaces.

Blood Group Structures

Blood Group Surface Carbohydrate Additional Sugar
O Trisaccharide (base structure) None

Transfusion Compatibility

Clinical Application

Universal Donor vs. Universal Recipient

Type O: Universal Donor

  • Can donate to: O, A, B, AB (everyone)
  • Can receive from: O only
  • Reason: No A or B antigens to trigger immune response

Type AB: Universal Recipient

  • Can receive from: O, A, B, AB (everyone)
  • Can donate to: AB only
  • Reason: Has both antigens, so no immune rejection
The Rh Factor (+/-)

The "+" or "-" designation (e.g., O+, A-) refers to a second marker system (Rh factor) independent of the ABO carbohydrate antigens. This adds another layer of compatibility requirements.

Biotechnology Application: Universal Blood

Recent research has developed efficient enzymes to convert A, B, and AB blood into type O (universal donor):

  • Source: Enzymes from gut bacteria (glycosidases)
  • Function: Remove A and B antigens from red blood cells
  • Efficiency: 30√ó more efficient than previous methods
  • Discovery method: Metagenomics screening
Clinical Impact

This technology could revolutionize blood banking by:

  • Converting all blood supplies to universal donor type
  • Eliminating blood type matching requirements in emergencies
  • Reducing blood shortages
  • Improving disaster response capabilities
Research Highlight

Steven Withers Lab (University of British Columbia)

Key achievements:

  • Used metagenomics to screen millions of bacterial genes
  • Identified highly specific glycosidases for A and B antigens
  • Applied protein engineering to enhance enzyme efficiency
  • Created enzymes that completely remove antigens (critical for immune safety)
Why Efficiency Matters

Incomplete removal of A or B antigens would trigger immune responses in recipients. The enzyme must be highly efficient to ensure complete conversion and patient safety.

Key Takeaways

Enzyme Catalysis

Enzymes lower activation energy and accelerate reactions by 10‚Å∂-10¬π‚Å∞ fold. The entire protein structure participates in catalysis, not just the active site.

Thermodynamics

Exergonic reactions (ŒîG < 0) are favorable; endergonic reactions (ŒîG > 0) are unfavorable but can be driven by coupling to favorable reactions.

Metabolic Pathways

Sequential reactions solve equilibrium problems through coupling. Enzymes are co-localized for efficiency and regulation.

Feedback Regulation

End products inhibit the first enzyme in biosynthetic pathways (negative feedback), preventing overproduction and conserving resources.
